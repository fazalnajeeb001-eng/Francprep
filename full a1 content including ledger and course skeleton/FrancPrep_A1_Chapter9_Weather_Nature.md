# Warm-Up
Weather talk is universal small talk — you already know *il y a* (Chapter 6) and *il est* (Chapter 7). Today you'll meet a new, closely related use of *il*: describing the weather itself.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French describes weather using **impersonal *il*** — a subject that doesn't refer to any specific person or thing, just like English "it" in "it's raining." **Il fait** + adjective describes general conditions: **il fait beau** ("it's nice out"), **il fait chaud** ("it's hot"), **il fait froid** ("it's cold"). **Il pleut** ("it's raining") and **il neige** ("it's snowing") are their own fixed verb forms. **Il y a du vent** ("it's windy," literally "there is wind") and **il y a du soleil** ("it's sunny") reuse *il y a* from Chapter 6 in a new context.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| il fait beau | it's nice out | eel feh BOH | Aujourd'hui, il fait beau. |
| il fait chaud | it's hot | eel feh SHOH | En été, il fait chaud. |
| il fait froid | it's cold | eel feh FRWAH | En hiver, il fait froid. |
| il pleut | it's raining | eel PLUH | Il pleut aujourd'hui. |
| il neige | it's snowing | eel NEZH | Il neige en montagne. |
| il y a du vent | it's windy | eel ee ah dew VAHN | Il y a du vent aujourd'hui. |
| il y a du soleil | it's sunny | eel ee ah dew soh-LAY | Il y a du soleil ce matin. |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces impersonal *il* expressions for weather.

**Formation:**
*Il fait* + adjective (beau/chaud/froid). *Il pleut* / *il neige* (fixed forms). *Il y a* + du/de la + noun (vent/soleil).

**Usage:** Impersonal *il* never refers to a person — it's purely structural, the same way "it" works in "it's raining" in English.

**Examples:**
1. Aujourd'hui, il fait beau et il y a du soleil.
2. En hiver, il neige souvent dans les Alpes.
3. Il pleut, alors je reste à la maison.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Le temps fait beau* → ✅ *Il fait beau* (impersonal *il*, not *le temps* as the subject in this expression).
- ❌ Confusing *il fait froid* (weather) with a personal statement — this lesson's expressions describe conditions, not how a person feels (that contrast comes in Lesson 2).

**Mini Drills:**

2. Say "it's cold": __________

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Seasons & Temperature
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Discuss temperature and distinguish weather conditions from personal sensations.
**Grammar Focus:** *Il fait chaud/froid* (weather) vs. *j'ai chaud/froid* (personal sensation) — a genuine, important contrast.
**Vocabulary Focus:** un degré, il fait... degrés, j'ai chaud, j'ai froid
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know *il fait chaud* describes the weather. Today you'll learn that describing how *you* feel uses a completely different verb — a real distinction worth getting right from the start.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Un degré** ("a degree") lets you be precise: **il fait vingt degrés** ("it's 20 degrees"). But to describe your own physical sensation of temperature, French uses **avoir**, not *être* or *faire*: **j'ai chaud** ("I'm hot" — literally "I have hot"), **j'ai froid** ("I'm cold"). This is genuinely different from *il fait chaud* — the weather can be hot (*il fait chaud*) while you personally feel cold (*j'ai froid*), and vice versa.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| un degré | a degree | uh duh-GRAY | Il fait vingt-cinq degrés. |
| il fait... degrés | it's... degrees | eel feh ... duh-GRAY | Il fait dix degrés aujourd'hui. |
| j'ai chaud | I'm hot | zhay SHOH | J'ai chaud, ouvre la fenêtre ! |
| j'ai froid | I'm cold | zhay FRWAH | J'ai froid, ferme la porte. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the crucial contrast between weather description and personal sensation.

**Formation:**
| Meaning | Verb | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Weather condition | il fait + adjective | Il fait chaud. (The weather is hot.) |
| Personal sensation | avoir + chaud/froid | J'ai chaud. (I feel hot.) |

**Usage:** These can genuinely contradict each other in the same conversation: *Il fait froid dehors, mais j'ai chaud !* ("It's cold outside, but I feel hot!" — perhaps from exercise, a fever, etc.)

**Examples:**
1. Il fait trente degrés — j'ai très chaud !
2. Il fait froid, mais elle n'a pas froid ; elle porte un gros manteau.
3. Quelle température fait-il aujourd'hui ?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je suis chaud* (using *être*) → ✅ *J'ai chaud* (using *avoir*) — a genuinely common error, since English uses "to be."
- ❌ Confusing which sentence describes the weather vs. the person — always check which subject (*il* or *je/tu/il-she as a person*) is being used.

**Mini Drills:**
1. Say "I'm cold" (personal): __________
2. Say "it's 15 degrees" (weather): __________

# Warm-Up
Since Chapter 4, you've used *aller au travail/à l'école* as fixed phrases. Today, *aller* finally gets its full grammatical due — and with it, you gain a whole new way to talk about the future.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Aller** ("to go") is a genuinely irregular verb. Its full present tense:

| Pronoun | Aller |
|---|---|
| je | vais |
| tu | vas |
| il / elle | va |
| nous | allons |
| vous | allez |
| ils / elles | vont |

The **futur proche** ("near future") uses *aller* (conjugated) + an infinitive to describe something about to happen or planned: **je vais manger** ("I'm going to eat"), **il va pleuvoir** ("it's going to rain" — you've already seen this in Lesson 3's preview!), **nous allons voyager** ("we're going to travel").

---

# Vocabulary

*(This lesson is grammar-focused; no new standalone vocabulary items beyond the verb forms above.)*

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson formally teaches *aller*'s complete conjugation and its use in the futur proche.

**Formation:**
*Aller* (conjugated) + infinitive.
- *Je vais manger.* (I'm going to eat.)
- *Tu vas voyager.* (You're going to travel.)
- *Il/elle va pleuvoir.* (It's going to rain.)
- *Nous allons partir.* (We're going to leave.)
- *Vous allez adorer ça !* (You're going to love that!)
- *Ils/elles vont arriver bientôt.* (They're going to arrive soon.)

**Usage:** The futur proche is the most common, natural way to talk about near-future plans in everyday spoken French — more frequent in conversation than the full future tense (a separate structure taught at a later level).

**Examples:**
1. Demain, il va faire beau.
2. Nous allons faire une randonnée ce week-end.
3. Vous allez adorer cette saison !

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je vais à manger* (inserting *à*) → ✅ *Je vais manger* (no preposition between *aller* and the infinitive).
- ❌ *Il va faire beau* said with the wrong *aller* form: *Il vais faire beau* → ✅ *Il va faire beau* (match the conjugation to the subject).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: Nous __________ (aller) voyager cet été.
2. Complete: Elle __________ (aller) faire une randonnée.

# Lesson Explanation
France's climate genuinely varies by region: **la Bretagne** (Brittany, in the northwest) has a mild, rainy, ocean-influenced climate; **la Provence** (in the southeast) is warm and sunny, Mediterranean; **les Alpes** (the Alps) are cold and snowy, especially in winter. **Un climat** ("a climate") is the general word for this.

# Grammar Summary (Full Consolidated Reference)

**Impersonal weather expressions:** *il fait* + adjective; *il pleut*; *il neige*; *il y a du* + noun.

**Weather vs. sensation:** *il fait chaud/froid* (weather) vs. *j'ai chaud/froid* (personal sensation, using *avoir*).

**Aller (complete present tense):**
| Pronoun | Form |
|---|---|
| je | vais |
| tu | vas |
| il / elle | va |
| nous | allons |
| vous | allez |
| ils / elles | vont |

**Futur proche:** *aller* (conjugated) + infinitive, no preposition in between.
