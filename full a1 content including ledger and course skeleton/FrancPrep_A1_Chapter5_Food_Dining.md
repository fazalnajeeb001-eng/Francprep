# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to discuss food preferences, describe meals, and order confidently at a restaurant.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Name common foods and describe daily meals.
- Talk about fruits, vegetables, and drinks.
- Express taste preferences, distinguishing general likes from specific quantities.
- Order food and drink politely at a restaurant.
- Ask for the bill.
- Describe French meal customs.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can name common foods and describe my meals.
- I can talk about fruits, vegetables, and drinks I like.
- I can express food preferences clearly, using the right article.
- I can order at a restaurant politely.
- I can ask for the bill.
- I can describe basic French meal customs.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- **Partitive articles** (*du, de la, des, de l'*), built progressively across the chapter.
- **Definite vs. partitive article contrast** — *j'aime le chocolat* (general preference) vs. *je mange du chocolat* (a specific quantity) — a genuinely common point of confusion, addressed explicitly.
- **Je voudrais** ("I would like"), introduced as a fixed polite chunk — the full conditional mood is a B1+ structure; here it functions as ready-made, essential restaurant vocabulary.

**Vocabulary Domains:** Food, meals, fruits/vegetables, drinks, taste adjectives, restaurant expressions.

**Cultural Notes:** As previewed at the end of Chapter 4, French meal times tend to be more fixed and structured than in some other cultures, and lunch in particular is often a genuine, unhurried break. This chapter explores that directly in Lesson 6, alongside the traditional structure of a French meal (*entrée, plat principal, dessert*).

**Prerequisites:** Chapters 1–4. This chapter assumes full command of *être*, *avoir*, adjective agreement, the possessive adjective system, the *tu/vous* distinction, the complete reflexive verb paradigm, and the recurring cast introduced in Chapter 4.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can discuss food preferences naturally and handle a complete restaurant interaction, from ordering to paying.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# LESSON 1

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 1
**Lesson Title:** Basic Foods & Meals
**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Name common foods and describe daily meals, using the first partitive articles.
**Grammar Focus:** Partitive articles — introduction: *du* (masculine) and *de la* (feminine), meaning "some."
**Vocabulary Focus:** le petit-déjeuner, le déjeuner, le dîner, le pain, le fromage, la viande, le poisson
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
Think about what you ate for breakfast today. In French, talking about food you eat — rather than food in general — uses a special kind of article you haven't met yet. Today you'll learn it.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French has three daily meals: **le petit-déjeuner** (breakfast), **le déjeuner** (lunch), and **le dîner** (dinner).

To say you're eating "some" of something — an unspecified quantity, as opposed to the whole thing — French uses a **partitive article**: **du** before a masculine noun, **de la** before a feminine noun. *Je mange du pain* ("I'm eating [some] bread"), *je mange de la viande* ("I'm eating [some] meat"). This is different from saying *le pain* (bread in general, as a concept) — you'll explore that contrast directly in Lesson 3.

---

|---|---|---|---|
| le petit-déjeuner | breakfast | luh puh-tee day-zhuh-NAY | Le petit-déjeuner est à sept heures. |
| le déjeuner | lunch | luh day-zhuh-NAY | Le déjeuner est à midi. |
| le dîner | dinner | luh dee-NAY | Le dîner est à vingt heures. |
| le pain | bread | luh PAN | Je mange du pain le matin. |
| le fromage | cheese | luh froh-MAZH | J'aime le fromage français. |
| la viande | meat | lah VYAHND | Elle mange de la viande. |
| le poisson | fish | luh pwah-SOHN | Il mange du poisson le vendredi. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the first two forms of the partitive article.

**Formation (so far):**
| Gender | Partitive Article | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine | du | du pain |
| Feminine | de la | de la viande |

**Usage:** Use *du/de la* when talking about eating or having "some" of a food item — an unspecified amount, not the whole category.

**Examples:**
1. Je mange du pain et du fromage.
2. Awa mange de la viande, mais Camille préfère le poisson.
3. Au petit-déjeuner, je mange du pain.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je mange un pain.* → ✅ *Je mange du pain.* (unless referring to one whole loaf/roll specifically)
- ❌ Using *du* with a feminine noun: *du viande* → ✅ *de la viande.*

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: Je mange __________ pain. (du/de la)
2. Complete: Il mange __________ viande. (du/de la)

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Fruits, Vegetables & Drinks
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Talk about fruits, vegetables, and drinks, completing the partitive article system.
**Grammar Focus:** Partitive articles — completion: *des* (plural) and *de l'* (before a vowel sound).
**Vocabulary Focus:** les fruits, les légumes, l'eau, le café, le thé, le jus
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You know *du* and *de la* for "some." Today you'll complete the system — what happens with plural foods, and what happens when a food or drink starts with a vowel sound.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Des** is the partitive article for plural nouns (any gender): *des fruits* ("some fruits"), *des légumes* ("some vegetables") — the same form you already know as the plural indefinite article, now doing double duty.

**De l'** is used before any singular noun beginning with a vowel sound, regardless of gender — the same elision pattern you've seen throughout the course: *de l'eau* ("some water"), *de l'ananas* ("some pineapple").

**Complete Partitive System:**
| Gender/Number | Article |
|---|---|
| Masculine singular | du |
| Feminine singular | de la |
| Before a vowel sound (either gender) | de l' |
| Plural (any gender) | des |

---

|---|---|---|---|
| les fruits | fruits | lay FRWEE | Je mange des fruits chaque jour. |
| les légumes | vegetables | lay lay-GEWM | Elle mange des légumes au dîner. |
| l'eau | water | LOH | Je bois de l'eau. |
| le café | coffee | luh kah-FAY | Il boit du café le matin. |
| le thé | tea | luh TAY | Elle préfère le thé. |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson completes the partitive article system.

**Formation (complete):**
| Gender/Number | Article | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular | du | du pain |
| Feminine singular | de la | de la viande |
| Vowel sound (either gender) | de l' | de l'eau |
| Plural | des | des fruits |

**Usage:** *Des* is identical in form to the plural indefinite article you may recognize from earlier chapters' vocabulary (*des chaussures*, Chapter 3) — context tells you whether it means "some [countable items]" or simply "[plural things]."

**Examples:**
1. Je bois de l'eau et du jus.
2. Nous mangeons des fruits et des légumes.
3. Léo boit du café ; Nora préfère le thé.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *de la eau* → ✅ *de l'eau* (elision before a vowel sound, regardless of gender).
- ❌ *du fruits* → ✅ *des fruits* (plural noun needs *des*, not the masculine singular *du*).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: Je bois __________ eau. (de la/de l')
2. Complete: Nous mangeons __________ légumes. (du/des)

# LESSON 3

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 3
**Lesson Title:** Taste & Preferences
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Express taste preferences, distinguishing general likes from specific quantities.
**Grammar Focus:** Definite article (*le/la/les*) vs. partitive article (*du/de la/des*) — expressing a general preference versus a specific quantity.
**Vocabulary Focus:** délicieux/délicieuse, bon/bonne, mauvais/mauvaise, sucré(e), salé(e)
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You've used *le/la/les* since early chapters (*le sport*, *la musique*) and *du/de la/des* since Lesson 1 of this chapter. Today, you'll learn exactly when to use each — a genuinely common point of confusion, made clear.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French uses **two different article systems** depending on what you mean:

- **Definite article (le/la/les)** for a general preference or category: **J'aime le chocolat** ("I like chocolate" — chocolate in general, as a category).
- **Partitive article (du/de la/des)** for a specific, unspecified quantity you're eating or drinking: **Je mange du chocolat** ("I'm eating some chocolate" — an actual portion, right now).

Compare: *J'aime le café* (I like coffee, in general) versus *Je bois du café* (I'm drinking some coffee, right now). The verb often signals which you need — *aimer/adorer/détester* usually pair with the definite article; *manger/boire* usually pair with the partitive.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| délicieux / délicieuse | delicious | day-lee-SYUH / day-lee-SYUHZ | Ce plat est délicieux. |
| bon / bonne | good | BOHN / BUN | Ce fromage est bon. |
| mauvais / mauvaise | bad | moh-VEH / moh-VEZ | Ce café est mauvais. |
| sucré / sucrée | sweet | sew-KRAY | J'aime les fruits sucrés. |
| salé / salée | salty | sah-LAY | Ce plat est très salé. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson formally names and contrasts a pattern you've been using implicitly since early chapters.

**Formation/Contrast:**
| Meaning | Article Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| General preference | Definite (le/la/les) | J'aime le chocolat. |
| Specific quantity | Partitive (du/de la/des) | Je mange du chocolat. |

**Usage:** This is not a free choice — the meaning genuinely changes. *J'aime le chocolat* describes a lasting preference; *je mange du chocolat* describes a present, specific action.

**Examples:**
1. J'adore les fruits, mais je ne mange pas de fruits aujourd'hui. *(note: "pas de" replaces the partitive after a negation — recognize this pattern for now, formally taught in a future chapter)*
2. Camille aime le café, et elle boit du café chaque matin.
3. Il déteste les légumes, mais il mange des légumes ce soir, pour sa santé.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *J'aime du chocolat.* → ✅ *J'aime le chocolat.* (General preference needs the definite article, not the partitive.)
- ❌ *Je mange le pain* (when meaning "I'm eating some bread right now") → ✅ *Je mange du pain.*

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: J'adore __________ chocolat. (general preference)
2. Complete: Je mange __________ chocolat maintenant. (specific quantity)

---

# LESSON 4

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 4
**Lesson Title:** Ordering at a Restaurant
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Order food and drink politely at a restaurant.
**Grammar Focus:** *Je voudrais* ("I would like") — introduced as a fixed polite chunk.
**Vocabulary Focus:** une table, la carte, le menu, l'entrée, le plat principal, le dessert
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know *je veux* is too direct for polite requests in most contexts — French has a softer, more polite way to say "I would like." Today, at Julien's café, you'll learn it.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Je voudrais** ("I would like") is the standard polite way to order or request something in French — softer and more courteous than *je veux* ("I want"). It comes from the conditional form of the irregular verb *vouloir*, a full grammar point reserved for a later level; here, treat **je voudrais** as a fixed, essential chunk, the same way *je m'appelle* was first introduced before its full grammar was explained.

A French restaurant meal traditionally has three parts: **l'entrée** (starter), **le plat principal** (main course), and **le dessert**. **La carte** or **le menu** is what you ask for to see what's available: *La carte, s'il vous plaît.*

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| une table | a table | ewn TAHBL | Une table pour deux, s'il vous plaît. |
| la carte | the menu | lah KART | La carte, s'il vous plaît. |
| le menu | the set menu | luh muh-NEW | Je prends le menu du jour. |
| l'entrée | the starter | lahn-TRAY | Je voudrais une salade en entrée. |
| le plat principal | the main course | luh plah pran-see-PAL | Le plat principal, c'est du poisson. |
| le dessert | dessert | luh day-SEHR | Je voudrais un dessert sucré. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces *je voudrais* as a fixed, polite chunk for ordering and requesting.

**Formation (chunk only, not full conjugation):**
*Je voudrais* + [noun] → "I would like [noun]."

**Usage:** *Je voudrais* is always more polite than *je veux* — use it by default in any restaurant, shop, or formal request context.

**Examples:**
1. Je voudrais une table pour deux, s'il vous plaît.
2. Je voudrais le plat principal avec des légumes.
3. Pour le dessert, je voudrais quelque chose de sucré.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je veux un café.* (too blunt in most polite contexts) → ✅ *Je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît.*
- ❌ Forgetting *s'il vous plaît* — *je voudrais* is already polite, but pairing it with *s'il vous plaît* is standard, warm practice, not redundant.

**Mini Drills:**
1. Politely request a table for two: __________ une table pour deux.
2. Politely order a coffee: __________ un café, s'il vous plaît.

# Lesson Explanation
