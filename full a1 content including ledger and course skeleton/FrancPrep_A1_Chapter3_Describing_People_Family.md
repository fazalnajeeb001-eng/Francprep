# Chapter 3 — Describing People & Family

---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to describe physical appearance and personality, talk about likes and dislikes, and introduce and describe their immediate family.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Describe someone's physical appearance, with adjectives correctly agreeing in gender and number.
- Describe someone's personality.
- Describe colors and clothing.
- Express likes and dislikes.
- Introduce and describe family members using possessive adjectives.
- Describe relationships between people.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can describe a person's physical appearance.
- I can describe a person's personality.
- I can describe what someone is wearing, including colors.
- I can say what I like and don't like.
- I can introduce my family members using the correct possessive adjective.
- I can describe how people are related to each other.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- **Adjective agreement**, extended from Chapter 1's gender-only pattern to include **plural agreement** (adding *-s*), plus the **placement rule** for color adjectives (after the noun).
- **Possessive adjectives**, introduced progressively across the chapter: *mon/ma/mes* and *ton/ta/tes* → *son/sa/ses* → *notre/nos* and *votre/vos* → *leur/leurs*.
- A minimal, functional introduction to negation (*ne...pas*), taught only as much as needed to express dislikes clearly.

**Vocabulary Domains:** Physical descriptors, personality traits, colors and clothing, family members, relationship vocabulary.

**Cultural Notes:** French families are often described using slightly different structural terms than in English (e.g., *demi-frère*/*demi-sœur* for half-siblings, *beau-père*/*belle-mère* covering both "father/mother-in-law" and "stepfather/stepmother," depending on context) — FrancPrep introduces only the core, unambiguous family terms at A1, with these nuanced terms reserved for a later, more appropriate level.

**Prerequisites:** Chapter 1 (Greetings & First Contact) and Chapter 2 (Personal Information). This chapter assumes full command of *être* and *avoir* (all persons), gender-based adjective agreement, *tu/vous*, *est-ce que* and inversion questions, and the nationality/profession vocabulary already taught. None of this is re-taught here.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can describe themselves and other people — physically, in personality, and by family relationship — using correctly agreeing adjectives and the full system of possessive adjectives.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# LESSON 1

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 1
**Lesson Title:** Physical Description
**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Describe physical appearance, extending adjective agreement to include plural forms.
**Grammar Focus:** Adjective agreement — extension: plural agreement (adding *-s* for plural nouns, in addition to the gender agreement already known from Chapter 1).
**Vocabulary Focus:** grand(e), petit(e), mince, les cheveux, court(e), long/longue, blond(e), brun(e), les yeux, bleu(e), vert(e), marron
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to make an adjective feminine by adding *-e* (*content* → *contente*). Today you'll learn the second half of adjective agreement in French: what happens when you're describing more than one person or thing.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French adjectives agree not only in **gender** (which you know from Chapter 1) but also in **number**. To make an adjective plural, you typically add **-s**: *Il est grand* → *Ils sont grands*. If the adjective is already feminine, the *-s* is simply added after the existing *-e*: *Elle est petite* → *Elles sont petites*.

Physical descriptions in French commonly use **avoir** (not *être*) when describing hair and eyes: **Elle a les cheveux bruns** ("She has brown hair"), **Il a les yeux bleus** ("He has blue eyes") — the adjective still agrees with the noun it describes (*cheveux*, *yeux* — both masculine plural nouns), not with the person.

One color deserves special note: **marron** ("brown," for eyes) is **invariable** — it never changes form, regardless of gender or number: *les yeux marron* (no added -s, no added -e). This is a genuine exception you'll want to remember precisely.

---

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson extends the adjective agreement pattern from Chapter 1 (gender only) to include number (singular/plural).

**Formation:**
| | Masculine Singular | Masculine Plural | Feminine Singular | Feminine Plural |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| grand | grand | grands | grande | grandes |
| petit | petit | petits | petite | petites |
| bleu | bleu | bleus | bleue | bleues |

**Usage:** Add *-s* to make an adjective plural, whether it's masculine or feminine. If the adjective already ends in *-s* in the masculine singular (not the case for any word in this lesson, but a pattern to watch for later), no additional *-s* is added.

**Examples:**
1. Ils sont grands. (masculine plural)
2. Elles sont petites. (feminine plural)
3. Il a les yeux marron. (marron never changes)

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Elle a les yeux marrons.* → ✅ *Elle a les yeux marron.* (Marron is invariable — never add -s.)
- ❌ *Ils sont grand.* → ✅ *Ils sont grands.* (Don't forget the plural -s.)

**Mini Drills:**
1. Make plural: *Il est petit.* → Ils sont __________.
2. Complete correctly: *Elle a les yeux __________.* (brown, using the invariable word)

# LESSON 2

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Personality Traits
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Describe personality traits and use the first set of possessive adjectives.
**Grammar Focus:** Possessive adjectives — introduction: *mon/ma/mes* ("my") and *ton/ta/tes* ("your," informal).
**Vocabulary Focus:** gentil(le), drôle, sérieux/sérieuse, timide, sympathique, intelligent(e)
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You've described what people look like — now you'll describe what people are *like*. You'll also meet a new grammar tool: words for "my" and "your," which — like adjectives — change form depending on the noun that follows them.

---

# Lesson Explanation
Personality adjectives follow the same agreement rules you already know: **Il est gentil** / **Elle est gentille**; **Il est sérieux** / **Elle est sérieuse**. Some, like *timide* and *sympathique*, are **invariable** — they already end in *-e*, so no change is needed for the feminine form.

This lesson introduces **possessive adjectives** — French words for "my" and "your" that agree with the noun they describe, not with the speaker. **Mon** is used before a masculine singular noun, **ma** before a feminine singular noun, and **mes** before any plural noun: *mon frère* (my brother), *ma sœur* (my sister), *mes parents* (my parents). **Ton/ta/tes** follow the identical pattern for "your" (informal).

One small but important exception: before a feminine noun that begins with a vowel sound, **mon** is used instead of **ma**, purely for pronunciation ease: *mon amie* (not *ma amie*), even though *amie* is feminine.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| gentil / gentille | kind | zhahn-TEE / zhahn-TEEY | Elle est gentille. |
| drôle *(invariable)* | funny | DROHL | Il est drôle. |
| sérieux / sérieuse | serious | say-RYUH / say-RYUHZ | Elle est sérieuse. |
| timide *(invariable)* | shy | tee-MEED | Il est timide. |
| sympathique *(invariable)* | nice / friendly | san-pah-TEEK | Elle est sympathique. |
| intelligent / intelligente | intelligent | an-teh-lee-ZHAHN / an-teh-lee-ZHAHNT | Il est intelligent. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces possessive adjectives for the first and second person singular.

**Formation:**
| | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Plural (any gender) |
|---|---|---|---|
| my | mon | ma (mon before a vowel sound) | mes |
| your (informal) | ton | ta (ton before a vowel sound) | tes |

**Usage:** The possessive adjective always agrees with the noun that follows it — not with the gender of the owner. A man and a woman both say *ma sœur* (my sister), because *sœur* is feminine.

**Examples:**
1. Mon frère est drôle.
2. Ta sœur est sérieuse.
3. Mes parents sont sympathiques.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Ma amie* → ✅ *Mon amie* (vowel-sound exception).
- ❌ Matching the possessive to the owner's gender instead of the noun's: a woman incorrectly saying *ma frère* → ✅ *mon frère* (frère is masculine, regardless of who is speaking).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: __________ (my) sœur est gentille.
2. Complete: __________ (your, informal) frères sont drôles.

# LESSON 3

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 3
**Lesson Title:** Colors & Clothing
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Describe colors and clothing items, and understand adjective placement.
**Grammar Focus:** Adjective placement — color adjectives are placed **after** the noun they describe (unlike some other adjectives, which come before); recycled possessive adjectives.
**Vocabulary Focus:** une chemise, un pantalon, une robe, des chaussures, rouge, noir(e), blanc/blanche, porter
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
In English, an adjective almost always comes before the noun ("a blue shirt"). French does this too — but not always. Today you'll learn one of the clearest examples of a different word order: color adjectives.

---

# Lesson Explanation
Unlike in English, French color adjectives are placed **after** the noun they describe: **une chemise bleue** ("a blue shirt" — literally "a shirt blue"), not *"une bleue chemise."* This is a genuine and consistent rule for colors, and for most descriptive adjectives in French generally (a small set of very common short adjectives, like *grand* and *petit* from Lesson 1, are exceptions that come before the noun — you've already been using them correctly).

**Porter** ("to wear") is introduced here as a lexical chunk: **il/elle porte** ("he/she wears"), useful for describing clothing.

**Blanc/blanche** ("white") is a genuinely irregular adjective in its feminine form (not a simple added *-e* — the whole ending changes), worth noting explicitly as an exception to flag rather than derive by pattern.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| une chemise | a shirt | ewn shuh-MEEZ | Il porte une chemise bleue. |
| un pantalon | pants | uhn pahn-tah-LOHN | Elle porte un pantalon noir. |
| une robe | a dress | ewn ROB | Elle porte une robe rouge. |
| des chaussures | shoes | day shoh-SUR | Il porte des chaussures noires. |
| rouge *(invariable)* | red | ROOZH | Elle porte une robe rouge. |
| noir / noire | black | NWAHR / NWAHR | Il porte un pantalon noir. |
| blanc / blanche | white (irregular) | BLAHN / BLAHNSH | Elle porte une chemise blanche. |
| porter | to wear | por-TAY | Il porte une chemise. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces adjective placement, using color as the clearest example, and flags one important irregular adjective form.

**Formation — Placement:**
- ✅ *une chemise bleue* (noun + color adjective)
- ❌ *une bleue chemise*
- (Recall exceptions from earlier: *un grand homme*, where *grand* comes before the noun — these short, common adjectives are learned individually as exceptions.)

**Formation — Blanc/blanche (irregular feminine):**
| Masculine | Feminine |
|---|---|
| blanc | blanche |

**Usage:** Always place a color adjective after the noun; always agree it in gender and number (except *marron*, which stays invariable, and *rouge*, which is already the same in both genders since it ends in *-e*).

**Examples:**
1. Elle porte une robe blanche.
2. Ils portent des chaussures noires.
3. Il porte un pantalon rouge.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *une blanche chemise* → ✅ *une chemise blanche*
- ❌ *une chemise blance* → ✅ *une chemise blanche* (the whole ending changes, not just an added -e)

**Mini Drills:**
1. Correct the order: *une noire robe* → __________
2. Complete: *Il porte une chemise __________.* (white, feminine)

# LESSON 4

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 4
**Lesson Title:** Likes & Dislikes
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Express likes and dislikes, and use the third set of possessive adjectives.
**Grammar Focus:** Possessive adjectives — introduction: *son/sa/ses* ("his/her"); a minimal, functional introduction to negation (*ne...pas*) for expressing dislikes.
**Vocabulary Focus:** aimer (chunk), adorer (chunk), détester (chunk), le sport, la musique, les films, la lecture
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You've described people — now you'll describe what they like. You'll also complete an important pattern: how to say "his" or "her" in French, and how to say you *don't* like something.

---

# Lesson Explanation
To express likes, French uses **j'aime** ("I like"), **j'adore** ("I love/adore"), and **je déteste** ("I hate/detest") + a noun (usually with *le/la/les*, since French generally states the full category being liked): *J'aime le sport* ("I like sports"), *J'adore la musique* ("I love music").

To say you *don't* like something, French wraps the verb in **ne...pas**: **Je n'aime pas** ("I don't like"). Note that *ne* becomes *n'* before a vowel sound (as in *n'aime*). This is your first explicit introduction to French negation — you'll see this same *ne...pas* pattern used with other verbs throughout the course.

This lesson also introduces **son/sa/ses** ("his/her") — following the exact same pattern as *mon/ma/mes*, and agreeing with the noun, not the owner's gender: **son frère** can mean "his brother" *or* "her brother," depending on context, because *frère* is masculine either way.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| j'aime | I like | zhem | J'aime le sport. |
| j'adore | I love / adore | zhah-DOR | J'adore la musique. |
| je déteste | I hate / detest | zhuh day-TEST | Je déteste la pluie. |
| je n'aime pas | I don't like | zhuh nem PAH | Je n'aime pas les films d'horreur. |
| le sport | sport(s) | luh SPOR | J'aime le sport. |
| la musique | music | lah mew-ZEEK | Elle adore la musique. |
| les films | movies | lay FEELM | Il aime les films. |
| la lecture | reading | lah lek-TUR | J'aime la lecture. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson adds the third-person possessive adjectives and introduces basic negation.

**Formation — Possessives (his/her):**
| | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Plural (any gender) |
|---|---|---|---|
| his/her | son | sa (son before a vowel sound) | ses |

**Formation — Negation:**
*ne* + verb + *pas* → *n'* + verb + *pas* before a vowel sound.
- *Je n'aime pas* le café.
- *Il ne déteste pas* la musique classique.

| | Singular (any gender) | Plural (any gender) |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This final possessive lesson completes the entire chapter's grammar system.

**Formation:**
| | Singular (any gender) | Plural (any gender) |
|---|---|---|
| their | leur | leurs |

**Full Possessive System (complete, all persons):**
| | Masc. Sing. | Fem. Sing. | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| my | mon | ma | mes |
| your (informal) | ton | ta | tes |
| his/her | son | sa | ses |

| your (formal/plural) | votre | votre | vos |

**Common Mistakes:**

"Their grandmother is kind" is:
a) Leur grand-mère est gentil b) Leurs grand-mère est gentille c) Leur grand-mère est gentille d) Leur grand-mère est gentil

**2. Matching**
1. Notre — a) Their
2. Leur — b) Our
3. Son — c) His/her

**3. Fill in the Blank**
Elle porte une robe __________. (red)

**4. Sentence Ordering**
(a) Mon frère est grand. (b) Voici ma famille. (c) Il a les yeux bleus.

**5. Translation**
Translate into French: "My sister is kind and funny. She doesn't like sports, but she loves music."

**6. Short Answer**
Explain, using an example, why *marron* and *blanc* are both exceptions to the regular adjective pattern — but different kinds of exceptions.

**7. Communicative Practice**
Describe a family member aloud (recorded or with a partner) for 1 minute, covering appearance, personality, and one like/dislike, using at least one possessive adjective.

**Answer Key:**
1. c) Leur grand-mère est gentille
2. 1-b, 2-a, 3-c
3. rouge
4. b, a, c
5. Ma sœur est gentille et drôle. Elle n'aime pas le sport, mais elle adore la musique.
6. Sample answer: *Marron* is invariable — it never changes at all, in any context. *Blanc* is irregular but does change — its feminine form (*blanche*) simply doesn't follow the normal "just add -e" pattern.
7. (Open-ended — assess for range, accuracy, and correct possessive/agreement use.)
