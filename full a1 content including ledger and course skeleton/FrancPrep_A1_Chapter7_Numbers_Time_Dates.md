---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to tell time, discuss days/months/seasons, make appointments, and talk about dates and schedules.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Tell time formally, using the 24-hour clock.
- Name the days of the week and express recurring routines.
- Name the months and seasons.
- Make and negotiate an appointment, using informal time.
- Give and understand dates, including birthdays.
- Discuss a schedule using the full system of time prepositions.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can tell the time formally.
- I can name the days of the week and describe a weekly routine.
- I can name the months and seasons.
- I can make an appointment, using informal time.
- I can give a date and talk about birthdays.
- I can discuss a schedule.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- **Formal time-telling** (24-hour clock: *il est quatorze heures*).
- **Le + day of the week** for habitual/recurring actions (*le lundi* = "on Mondays," generally) vs. a bare day name for one specific occurrence (*lundi* = "on [this coming] Monday").
- **En** for months and seasons (*en janvier*, *en été* — with one exception, *au printemps*).
- **Informal time-telling** (12-hour clock with *et quart / et demie / moins le quart*, plus *du matin/de l'après-midi/du soir* to disambiguate).
- **Dans** for future time expressions (*dans deux jours* — "in two days").

**Vocabulary Domains:** Clock time, days of the week, months, seasons, appointments, dates.

**Cultural Notes:** The 24-hour clock is the default in France for anything official — transport schedules, TV listings, business hours — while the 12-hour clock with *du matin/du soir* remains completely normal in casual, everyday speech. Both are genuinely current, not one being "more correct" than the other.

**Prerequisites:** Chapters 1–6. This chapter assumes full command of numbers 0–100 (Chapters 2 and 6), which it uses extensively for both clock time and dates, without re-teaching them.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can tell time in both registers, discuss recurring schedules, and make an appointment confidently.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Tell time formally, using the 24-hour clock.

**Vocabulary Focus:** quelle heure est-il, il est, une heure, pile, environ
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know all the numbers you need for this lesson (0–100, from Chapters 2 and 6). Today you'll put them to a new use: telling time the way French uses it in any official or written context.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Quelle heure est-il ?** ("What time is it?") is answered with **Il est** + number + **heure(s)**: **Il est une heure** ("It's one o'clock" — note *une heure*, not *un heure*, since *heure* is feminine), **Il est quatorze heures** ("It's 2:00 PM," literally "fourteen hours").

France uses the **24-hour clock** by default for schedules, transport, TV listings, and business hours — *quatorze heures* unambiguously means 2:00 PM, with no need for "AM/PM." Minutes are simply added after: **il est quatorze heures trente** ("it's 2:30 PM").

**Pile** ("exactly," as in "on the dot") and **environ** ("around, approximately") add precision: *Il est quinze heures pile* / *Il est quinze heures environ.*

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| quelle heure est-il | what time is it | kel UR eh-TEEL | Quelle heure est-il, s'il vous plaît ? |
| il est | it is (for time) | eel EH | Il est seize heures. |
| une heure | one o'clock / an hour | ewn UR | Il est une heure. |
| pile | exactly / on the dot | PEEL | Il est midi pile. |
| environ | around / approximately | ahn-vee-ROHN | Il est dix-huit heures environ. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces formal time-telling using the 24-hour clock, building directly on numbers you already know.

**Formation:**
*Il est* + [number 0–23] + *heure(s)* (+ minutes, if any).
- *Il est une heure.* (1:00) — singular *heure*, no -s.
- *Il est deux heures.* (2:00) — plural *heures*, with -s.
- *Il est quatorze heures trente.* (14:30 / 2:30 PM)

**Usage:** *Midi* (noon) and *minuit* (midnight) are special fixed words, not "douze heures" — recognize and use these directly.

**Examples:**
1. Il est neuf heures.
2. Il est vingt et une heures quinze.
3. Il est midi pile.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Il est un heure.* → ✅ *Il est une heure.* (*Heure* is feminine, so it takes *une*, not *un*.)
- ❌ *Il est douze heures* (for noon) → ✅ *Il est midi.*

**Mini Drills:**
1. Say 3:00 PM in the 24-hour format: __________
2. Say noon: __________

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Days of the Week
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Name the days of the week and express recurring routines.
**Grammar Focus:** *Le* + day of the week for habitual/recurring actions, versus a bare day name for one specific occurrence.
**Vocabulary Focus:** lundi, mardi, mercredi, jeudi, vendredi, samedi, dimanche, le week-end, un jour
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to describe habits using frequency adverbs (Chapter 4). Today you'll learn a second way French expresses "regularly" — this time, attached directly to a day of the week.

---

# Lesson Explanation
The seven days: **lundi** (Monday), **mardi** (Tuesday), **mercredi** (Wednesday), **jeudi** (Thursday), **vendredi** (Friday), **samedi** (Saturday), **dimanche** (Sunday). **Le week-end** covers Saturday and Sunday together.

Here's the key grammar point: **lundi** alone means "on [this coming/that specific] Monday" — a one-time event: *Je travaille lundi* ("I'm working this Monday"). Adding **le** — **le lundi** — means "on Mondays, generally" — a recurring habit: *Je travaille le lundi* ("I work on Mondays," every week).

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| lundi | Monday | luhn-DEE | Je travaille le lundi. |
| mardi | Tuesday | mar-DEE | Mardi, j'ai un rendez-vous. |
| mercredi | Wednesday | mehr-kruh-DEE | Le mercredi, Nora n'a pas cours. |
| jeudi | Thursday | zhuh-DEE | Jeudi prochain, c'est son anniversaire. |
| vendredi | Friday | vahn-druh-DEE | Le vendredi soir, on se détend. |
| samedi | Saturday | sam-DEE | Samedi, je fais les courses. |
| dimanche | Sunday | dee-MAHNSH | Le dimanche, on déjeune en famille. |
| le week-end | the weekend | luh wee-KEND | Le week-end, je me repose. |
| un jour | a day | uhn ZHOOR | Quel jour sommes-nous ? |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the contrast between a bare day name (specific occurrence) and *le* + day name (habitual recurrence).

**Formation:**
| Meaning | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| One specific occurrence | [day], no article | Je travaille lundi. (this Monday) |
| Recurring habit | le + [day] | Je travaille le lundi. (every Monday) |

**Usage:** This exact contrast mirrors the frequency-adverb distinction from Chapter 4 (*toujours/souvent*, etc.) — both are ways of expressing how often something happens, just using a different grammatical tool.

**Examples:**
1. Le samedi, Madame Lambert fait les courses. (every Saturday)
2. Samedi, elle va chez sa fille. (this specific Saturday)
3. Le week-end, nous nous détendons.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ Using *le lundi* when you mean one specific Monday → ✅ Drop the article: *lundi.*
- ❌ Forgetting *le* when describing a genuine weekly habit → ✅ *Le lundi, je travaille* (not just *lundi, je travaille*, if you mean every week).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Say "on Sundays" (habitual): __________ dimanche.
2. Say "this Thursday" (specific): __________

# Warm-Up

---

# Lesson Explanation
The twelve months: **janvier, février, mars, avril, mai, juin, juillet, août, septembre, octobre, novembre, décembre.** None are capitalized in French, unlike English.

To say "in [month]," use **en**: **en janvier**, **en juillet**. The same word works for three of the four seasons: **en été** (in summer), **en automne** (in autumn), **en hiver** (in winter). The exception is spring: **au printemps** (not *"en printemps"*) — a genuine irregularity to memorize directly.

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces *en* for months and three of the four seasons, with *au printemps* as the one flagged exception.

**Formation:**
| Time Expression | Preposition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Months | en | en janvier |
| Summer/autumn/winter | en | en été / en automne / en hiver |
| Spring (exception) | au | au printemps |

**Usage:** There's no reliable pattern explaining why spring is different — it's simply memorized as the one exception in an otherwise consistent system.

**Examples:**
1. Mon anniversaire est en septembre.
2. En hiver, il fait très froid.
3. Au printemps, les fleurs poussent.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *En printemps* → ✅ *Au printemps* (the one true exception).
- ❌ Capitalizing month names: *Mars* → ✅ *mars* (lowercase, unlike English).

# LESSON 4

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 4
**Lesson Title:** Making Appointments
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Make and negotiate an appointment, using informal time.
**Grammar Focus:** Informal time-telling — 12-hour clock with *et quart / et demie / moins le quart*, plus *du matin/de l'après-midi/du soir* to disambiguate.
**Vocabulary Focus:** et quart, et demie, moins le quart, du matin, de l'après-midi, du soir, prendre rendez-vous, être libre
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You know the formal, 24-hour way to tell time. In everyday conversation between friends, French usually uses a different, more casual system — today you'll learn it, right where it matters most: making plans.

---

# Lesson Explanation
In casual speech, French typically uses a **12-hour clock** with fraction words: **et quart** ("quarter past," +15), **et demie** ("half past," +30), **moins le quart** ("quarter to," -15): **il est trois heures et quart** (3:15), **il est trois heures et demie** (3:30), **il est quatre heures moins le quart** (3:45, literally "four o'clock minus the quarter").

Because the 12-hour clock is ambiguous on its own, add **du matin** (in the morning), **de l'après-midi** (in the afternoon), or **du soir** (in the evening) to clarify: *trois heures de l'après-midi* (3:00 PM).

**Prendre rendez-vous** ("to make an appointment") and **être libre** ("to be free/available") are essential for scheduling: *Vous êtes libre mardi ?*

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| et quart | quarter past | ay KAR | Il est deux heures et quart. |
| et demie | half past | ay duh-MEE | Il est deux heures et demie. |
| moins le quart | quarter to | mwan luh KAR | Il est trois heures moins le quart. |
| du matin | in the morning (clarifier) | dew mah-TAN | Il est neuf heures du matin. |
| de l'après-midi | in the afternoon (clarifier) | duh lah-preh-mee-DEE | Il est trois heures de l'après-midi. |
| du soir | in the evening (clarifier) | dew SWAHR | Il est huit heures du soir. |
| prendre rendez-vous *(chunk)* | to make an appointment | prahndr rahn-day-VOO | Je voudrais prendre rendez-vous. |
| être libre | to be free/available | ehtr LEEBR | Vous êtes libre mardi ? |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the informal, 12-hour time system, contrasted directly with Lesson 1's formal system.

**Formation:**
| Formal (24-hour) | Informal (12-hour) |
|---|---|
| quatorze heures | deux heures de l'après-midi |
| quatorze heures quinze | deux heures et quart de l'après-midi |
| quatorze heures trente | deux heures et demie de l'après-midi |
| quatorze heures quarante-cinq | trois heures moins le quart de l'après-midi |

**Usage:** Both systems are equally correct French — the choice depends on context (formal/written vs. casual/spoken), not on one being more advanced than the other.

**Examples:**
1. On se voit à trois heures et quart ?
2. Je suis libre à dix heures du matin.
3. Le rendez-vous est à quatre heures moins le quart de l'après-midi.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Trois heures et demi* → ✅ *Trois heures et demie* (*demie* agrees in gender with the feminine *heure*).
- ❌ Omitting *du matin/soir* when the time is genuinely ambiguous → ✅ Add it whenever confusion is possible.

**Mini Drills:**
1. Say 3:15 PM informally: __________
2. Say 7:45 in the evening informally: __________

**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Give and understand a date, and talk about birthdays, using the preposition *dans* for future time.

**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You've used *en* for months and *le* for recurring days. Today's preposition, *dans*, does something different — it points forward in time, from now.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Quelle est la date ?** or **C'est le combien ?** both ask "What's the date?" A full date is given as: *le* + number + month: **le douze mars** ("March 12th" — note French doesn't use an ordinal like "twelfth" the way English does, except for the 1st: *le premier janvier*).

**Dans** means "in [an amount of time from now]": **dans deux jours** ("in two days"), **dans une semaine** ("in a week"). This is different from *en*, which you already know is used for months/seasons, and different from *le*, used for recurring days — *dans* specifically measures forward from the present moment.

**Joyeux anniversaire !** ("Happy birthday!") and **fêter** ("to celebrate") complete this lesson's vocabulary.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| quelle est la date | what's the date | kel eh lah DAHT | Quelle est la date aujourd'hui ? |
| c'est le combien | what's the date (alternative) | seh luh kohm-byan | C'est le combien, aujourd'hui ? |
| dans *(preposition)* | in [amount of time] | DAHN | Mon anniversaire est dans une semaine. |
| joyeux anniversaire | happy birthday | zhwah-yuh zah-nee-vehr-SEHR | Joyeux anniversaire, Nora ! |
| fêter | to celebrate | feh-TAY | Nous fêtons son anniversaire samedi. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces *dans* for future time, contrasted directly with the other time prepositions taught so far in this chapter.

**Formation:**
*Dans* + [amount of time]: *dans trois jours*, *dans une semaine*, *dans un mois*.

**Usage — Full contrast of this chapter's time prepositions:**
| Preposition | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| en | months, most seasons | en juillet |
| au | spring (exception) | au printemps |
| le | recurring day | le lundi |
| dans | future time from now | dans deux jours |

**Examples:**
1. Mon anniversaire est dans un mois.
2. Dans deux semaines, c'est la rentrée.
3. Le douze mai, nous fêtons l'anniversaire de Madame Lambert.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *En deux jours* (meaning "in two days from now") → ✅ *Dans deux jours* (*en* is for months/seasons, not for counting forward from now).
- ❌ *Le deux mars* pronounced/written incorrectly with an ordinal ending → ✅ Only *le premier* uses an ordinal; all other dates use the plain number.
