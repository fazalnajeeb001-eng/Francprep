# FRANCPREP — LEVEL A1 (Découverte)
## Module A1.1 — Self & Others · Unit A1.1.1 — Greetings & Identity
# Chapter 2 — Personal Information

---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to exchange basic personal details: age, birthdate, origin, nationality, languages spoken, profession, and contact information.

**Communicative Goals:**
- State and ask for someone's age and birthdate.
- Say where you are from and ask others the same.
- State your nationality and the languages you speak.
- Describe your profession and ask about someone else's.
- Exchange contact details (phone number, email).
- Ask natural follow-up questions to keep a conversation going.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can say how old I am and ask someone their age.
- I can say where I am from and ask where someone else is from.
- I can state my nationality and the languages I speak.
- I can say what my job is and ask about someone else's.
- I can give and understand a phone number or email address.
- I can ask a simple follow-up question to continue a conversation naturally.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- The verb *avoir* (to have) in the present tense — introduced progressively across the chapter (j'ai / tu as → il/elle a → nous avons / vous avez → ils/elles ont).
- Question formation: consolidating *est-ce que* (from Chapter 1) and introducing basic inversion (*Avez-vous...?*, *Parlez-vous...?*).

**Vocabulary Domains:** Numbers 0–20, nationalities, professions, contact-information vocabulary.

**Cultural Notes:** In French, professions are stated without an article after *être* (*Je suis professeur*, not *Je suis un professeur*) unless the profession is modified by an adjective (*Je suis un bon professeur*). Phone numbers in France are typically read aloud in pairs of digits, not one digit at a time — a genuinely practical detail for real conversations.

**Prerequisites:** Chapter 1 — Greetings & First Contact. This chapter assumes full command of *être* (je, tu, il/elle, nous, vous), *s'appeler*, the *tu/vous* distinction, basic *est-ce que* questions, and core courtesy expressions. None of this is re-taught; it is used freely throughout.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can hold a complete, natural "getting to know you" exchange in French — covering age, origin, nationality, profession, and contact details — in either register.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# LESSON 1

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 1
**Lesson Title:** Age & Birthdate
**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask and state age and birthdate using numbers 0–20.
**Grammar Focus:** *Avoir* — introduction: *j'ai* / *tu as*, used in the fixed pattern *j'ai [number] ans* ("I am [number] years old").
**Vocabulary Focus:** Numbers 0–20; quel âge as-tu / avez-vous, j'ai... ans, ma date de naissance, né(e) le
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to say *je suis* and *je m'appelle*. Today you'll meet a new, equally essential verb — *avoir* ("to have") — which French uses in a place English wouldn't expect: to say your age. In French, you don't *are* a certain age — you *have* a certain age.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French uses *avoir* + a number + *ans* to state age: **J'ai vingt ans** literally means "I have twenty years," but simply translates as "I am twenty years old." This is different from English, where age uses "to be" — a genuine, useful contrast to notice early.

To ask someone's age: **Quel âge as-tu ?** (informal) or **Quel âge avez-vous ?** (formal) — both mean "How old are you?"

To talk about a birthdate: **Je suis né le...** (a man) / **Je suis née le...** (a woman) followed by a date, or simply **Ma date de naissance est le...** ("My birthdate is...").

# Grammar

**Explanation:** *Avoir* is one of the two most important verbs in French (alongside *être*), and — like *être* — it is irregular. This lesson introduces only *j'ai* and *tu as*, used specifically for stating age.

**Formation (so far):**
| Pronoun | Avoir |
|---|---|
| je | j'ai |
| tu | tu as |

**Usage:** *J'ai* + number + *ans*. Note that *je ai* is never written or said — it always contracts to **j'ai**.

**Examples:**
1. J'ai dix-huit ans.
2. Tu as quel âge ?
3. J'ai vingt ans, et toi ?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je suis vingt ans.* → ✅ *J'ai vingt ans.* (Age uses *avoir*, never *être*.)
- ❌ *J'ai vingt.* → ✅ *J'ai vingt ans.* (Never drop *ans*.)

**Mini Drills:**
1. State that you are sixteen: J'ai __________ ans.
2. Ask a friend their age: Quel âge __________ ?

# LESSON 2

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Where Are You From?
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask and state where someone is from.
**Grammar Focus:** No new verb — recycles *être* (Chapter 1) combined with the question word *d'où*.
**Vocabulary Focus:** d'où, je suis de, je viens de *(recognized only)*, une ville, un pays
**Estimated Time:** 20–25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You've already met *être* many times. Today you'll use it for a new purpose: talking about where someone comes from — a natural next question after names and ages in any first conversation.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**D'où** means "from where." Combined with *être*, it forms one of the most useful questions in early conversation: **D'où es-tu ?** (informal) or **D'où êtes-vous ?** (formal) — "Where are you from?"

To answer, use **Je suis de** + [city]: *Je suis de Lyon.* For a country, French sometimes requires a different small word (*Je suis du Canada*, *Je suis de France*) — this fuller pattern will be refined in a later chapter; for now, focus on the city-based pattern, which is fully regular.

You may also hear **Je viens de** ("I come from") used the same way — recognize it in listening, but you are not yet expected to produce it, since its verb (*venir*) is introduced formally later in the course.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| d'où | from where | doo | D'où es-tu ? |
| je suis de | I am from | zhuh swee duh | Je suis de Marseille. |
| je viens de *(receptive)* | I come from | zhuh vyan duh | Je viens de Dakar. |
| une ville | a city | ewn VEEL | Paris est une grande ville. |
| un pays | a country | uhn pay-EE | La France est un pays. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** No new verb is introduced. This lesson recycles the full present tense of *être* from Chapter 1 and adds a single new question word, *d'où*, following the *est-ce que* pattern you already know.

**Formation:**
- *D'où es-tu ?* / *D'où est-ce que tu es ?* (both correct — the second simply adds *est-ce que* for extra clarity)
- *D'où êtes-vous ?* / *D'où est-ce que vous êtes ?*

**Usage:** *D'où* always comes first in the question, before the verb or before *est-ce que*.

**Examples:**
1. D'où es-tu ? — Je suis de Nice.
2. D'où êtes-vous, Madame ? — Je suis de Bruxelles.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Tu es d'où ?* is heard casually in speech but ✅ *D'où es-tu ?* is the standard, safer form to produce at this level.
- ❌ *Je suis Lyon.* → ✅ *Je suis de Lyon.* (Never drop *de*.)

**Mini Drills:**
1. Ask a friend where they're from: __________ es-tu ?
2. Answer that you are from Montreal: Je suis __________ Montréal.

# LESSON 3

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 3
**Lesson Title:** Nationality & Languages
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** State nationality with correct adjective agreement and name languages spoken.
**Grammar Focus:** No new verb — recycles *être* + adjective agreement (Chapter 1) applied to nationalities; *parler* introduced only as a fixed lexical chunk (*je parle*), not yet formally conjugated.
**Vocabulary Focus:** français(e), canadien(ne), américain(e), sénégalais(e), une langue, je parle, un peu de
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to make an adjective feminine by adding *-e* (like *content* → *contente*). Nationalities follow this exact same pattern — today you're applying a rule you already know to brand-new vocabulary.

---

# Lesson Explanation
Nationalities in French are adjectives, and they agree with the speaker's gender, exactly like *content/contente* from Chapter 1: **Je suis français** (a man) / **Je suis française** (a woman). Nationality adjectives are not capitalized in French, unlike English (*français*, not *Français*, when used as an adjective).

To talk about languages, use the fixed expression **je parle** ("I speak") + the language name: *Je parle français.* You are not yet learning the full conjugation of *parler* — that comes in a later chapter on regular *-er* verbs. For now, treat *je parle* as a ready-made phrase, the same way you first used *je m'appelle* before learning reflexive verbs in full.

**Un peu de** ("a little") can soften a claim: *Je parle un peu d'anglais.*

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| français / française | French | frahn-SEH / frahn-SEZ | Elle est française. |
| canadien / canadienne | Canadian | kah-nah-DYAN / kah-nah-DYEN | Il est canadien. |
| américain / américaine | American | ah-may-ree-KAN / ah-may-ree-KEN | Je suis américaine. |
| sénégalais / sénégalaise | Senegalese | say-nay-gah-LEH / say-nay-gah-LEZ | Il est sénégalais. |
| une langue | a language | ewn LAHNG | Le français est une langue magnifique. |

| un peu de | a little (of) | uhn puh duh | Je parle un peu d'espagnol. |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** No new grammatical structure is introduced. This lesson deliberately recycles adjective agreement, applying the same *-e* pattern from Chapter 1 to a new vocabulary domain — a direct example of FrancPrep's spiral design.

**Formation (recap, applied to nationalities):**
- Masculine: *Il est canadien.*
- Feminine: *Elle est canadienne.* (note the doubled "n" before adding "-e" for nationalities ending in *-ien*)

**Usage:** Always match the nationality adjective to your own gender when speaking about yourself.

**Examples:**
1. Je suis française, et je parle français et un peu d'anglais.
2. Il est sénégalais ; elle est américaine.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ A woman saying *Je suis français.* → ✅ *Je suis française.*
- ❌ Capitalizing the adjective: *Je suis Français.* → ✅ *Je suis français.* (lowercase when used as an adjective)

**Mini Drills:**
1. A woman states she is Canadian: Je suis __________.
2. Say you speak a little English: Je parle __________ anglais.

# LESSON 4

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 4
**Lesson Title:** Professions & Jobs
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** State your profession and ask about someone else's, and extend *avoir* to the third person.
**Grammar Focus:** *Avoir* — extension: *il/elle a*; the "no article after *être* + profession" rule.
**Vocabulary Focus:** un métier, professeur(e), médecin, ingénieur(e), étudiant(e), qu'est-ce que tu fais dans la vie
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
Think about how you'd describe your job in one sentence in your own language. French has a small but important grammar quirk here that's different from English — pay close attention to the "no article" rule below.

---

# Lesson Explanation
To state a profession, French uses *être* + the profession — **with no article**: **Je suis professeur** (not *"Je suis un professeur"*). This is different from English, where "a" is required ("I am *a* teacher").

To ask about someone's job casually, French often uses: **Qu'est-ce que tu fais dans la vie ?** ("What do you do for a living?" — literally "What do you do in life?").

This lesson also extends *avoir* to the third person: **il a** / **elle a** ("he has" / "she has"), useful for describing someone else's job or possessions: *Elle a un métier intéressant* ("She has an interesting job").

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| un métier | a job / occupation | uhn may-TYAY | Elle a un métier intéressant. |
| professeur / professeure | teacher | proh-feh-SUR | Je suis professeur. |
| médecin | doctor | mayd-SAN | Il est médecin. |
| ingénieur / ingénieure | engineer | an-zhay-NYUR | Elle est ingénieure. |
| étudiant / étudiante | student | ay-tu-DYAHN / ay-tu-DYAHNT | Je suis étudiante. |
| qu'est-ce que tu fais dans la vie | what do you do for a living | kess kuh tu feh dahn lah vee | Qu'est-ce que tu fais dans la vie ? |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson adds the third-person forms of *avoir*, and formally names a rule you'll now apply consistently: no article before an unmodified profession noun after *être*.

**Formation:**
| Pronoun | Avoir |
|---|---|
| je | j'ai |
| tu | tu as |
| il / elle | il/elle a |

**Usage — the "no article" rule:**
- ✅ *Je suis médecin.* (no article)
- ✅ *Je suis un bon médecin.* (article appears *only* once an adjective like *bon* modifies the noun)
- ❌ *Je suis un médecin.* (incorrect at A1 — avoid this pattern)

**Examples:**
1. Elle a un métier fascinant.
2. Il est ingénieur ; elle est professeure.
3. Il a trente ans et il est étudiant.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je suis un professeur.* → ✅ *Je suis professeur.*
- ❌ *Elle à un métier.* → ✅ *Elle a un métier.* (Don't confuse *a* [has] with *à* [to/at] — they sound identical but are spelled differently and serve different grammatical roles.)

**Mini Drills:**
1. State that he has an interesting job: Il __________ un métier intéressant.
2. Say "I am a doctor" (no article): Je suis __________.

# Lesson Information

**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask natural follow-up questions using inversion, and complete the *avoir* paradigm.
**Grammar Focus:** Basic inversion question forms (*Avez-vous...?*, *Parlez-vous...?*); *avoir* — final extension: *ils/elles ont*.
**Vocabulary Focus:** et vous/toi, vraiment, ah bon, c'est intéressant, pourquoi
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
A real conversation rarely stops after one question and one answer — it keeps going. This lesson teaches you how the French you already know can be turned around to sound more natural, and how to keep a conversation alive.

---

# Lesson Explanation
So far, you've formed questions using *est-ce que* or rising intonation. French also commonly uses **inversion** — flipping the verb and pronoun, joined by a hyphen: **Avez-vous des enfants ?** ("Do you have children?") instead of *Est-ce que vous avez des enfants ?*. Inversion is common in more formal or written French; at A1, you only need to recognize and produce it with a small set of familiar verbs like *avoir* and *être*: **Êtes-vous...?**, **Avez-vous...?**, **Parlez-vous...?**

To keep a conversation going naturally, French uses short follow-up expressions: **Ah bon ?** ("Oh really?"), **Vraiment ?** ("Really?"), **C'est intéressant !** ("That's interesting!"), and **Pourquoi ?** ("Why?").

This lesson also completes *avoir*: **ils/elles ont** ("they have").

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| et vous / et toi | and you (recap, follow-up use) | ay VOO / ay TWAH | Ça va bien. Et vous ? |
| vraiment | really | vray-MAHN | Vraiment ? C'est super ! |
| ah bon | oh really | ah BOHN | Ah bon ? Je ne savais pas. |
| c'est intéressant | that's interesting | seh tan-tay-reh-SAHN | Ah, c'est intéressant ! |
| pourquoi | why | poor-KWAH | Pourquoi tu es fatigué ? |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces inversion questions and finishes the *avoir* paradigm for this chapter.

**Formation — Inversion (with familiar verbs only):**
| Statement | Inversion Question |
|---|---|
| Vous êtes française. | Êtes-vous française ? |
| Vous avez un numéro. | Avez-vous un numéro ? |
| Vous parlez anglais. | Parlez-vous anglais ? |

**Formation — Avoir (complete for this chapter):**
| Pronoun | Avoir |
|---|---|
| je | j'ai |
| tu | tu as |
| il / elle | il/elle a |
| nous | nous avons |
| vous | vous avez |
| ils / elles | ils/elles ont |

**Usage:** Inversion is somewhat more formal in tone than *est-ce que* — both are correct, and you can choose either, but recognizing inversion is essential since you'll encounter it often in formal writing and speech.

**Examples:**
1. Avez-vous des questions ?
2. Ils ont deux enfants.
3. Parlez-vous français couramment ?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Vous avez-vous...?* → ✅ *Avez-vous...?* (Don't repeat the pronoun.)
- ❌ Forgetting the hyphen in writing: *Avez vous* → ✅ *Avez-vous*

**Mini Drills:**

**Age pattern:** *avoir* + number + *ans* (not *être*).
**Profession pattern:** *être* + profession, no article (*je suis médecin*), unless modified by an adjective (*je suis un bon médecin*).
**Question formation (recap across two chapters):** rising intonation; *est-ce que* + statement; inversion with familiar verbs (*Avez-vous...?*, *Êtes-vous...?*, *Parlez-vous...?*).

# Mixed Practice Exercises (Comprehensive)

**1. Multiple Choice**
"He has an interesting job" is:
a) Il est un métier intéressant b) Il a un métier intéressant c) Il a métier intéressant d) Il est a un métier

**2. Matching**
1. Un pays — a) A language
2. Une langue — b) A country
3. Un métier — c) A job

**3. Fill in the Blank**
__________-vous français ? (parler, inversion)

**4. Sentence Ordering**
(a) J'ai vingt ans. (b) Quel âge as-tu ? (c) Je suis étudiante.

**5. Translation**
Translate into French: "Do you have a phone number? I am an engineer, and I speak a little French."

**6. Short Answer**
Explain, in your own words, why *avoir* rather than *être* is used to state age in French, using an example.

**7. Communicative Practice**
Hold a 1-minute conversation (recorded or with a partner) introducing yourself fully: name, age, origin, nationality, profession, and one follow-up question to the other person.

**Answer Key:**
1. b) Il a un métier intéressant
2. 1-b, 2-a, 3-c
3. Parlez
4. b, a, c
5. Avez-vous un numéro de téléphone ? Je suis ingénieur(e), et je parle un peu de français.
6. Sample answer: French treats age as something "possessed" rather than something one "is" — *j'ai vingt ans* literally means "I have twenty years," unlike the English "I am twenty."
7. (Open-ended — assess for range, accuracy, and inclusion of a genuine follow-up question.)
