## Module A1.1 — Self & Others · Unit A1.1.1 — Greetings & Identity
# Chapter 1 — Greetings & First Contact

---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to greet people appropriately for the time of day and social context, introduce themselves, ask and give basic personal information, and choose correctly between formal and informal address.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Greet someone appropriately depending on the time of day and the relationship between speakers.
- Introduce yourself and ask someone else's name.
- Ask and respond to "How are you?" in both formal and informal registers.
- Use core polite expressions naturally in everyday exchanges.
- Recognize when to use *tu* versus *vous*.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can say hello and goodbye appropriately for the time of day.
- I can introduce myself using my name.
- I can ask someone their name and understand their answer.
- I can ask how someone is doing and answer the same question.
- I can use basic courtesy expressions (please, thank you, excuse me).
- I can tell whether a situation calls for *tu* or *vous*.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- The verb *être* (to be) in the present tense — introduced progressively across the chapter (je suis / tu es → il/elle est → nous sommes / vous êtes → ils/elles sont).
- Basic question formation with *est-ce que* and rising intonation.
- The formal/informal address distinction: *tu* vs. *vous*.

**Vocabulary Domains:** Greetings and farewells, courtesy expressions, parts of the day, basic personal-information vocabulary (name, feelings/wellbeing).

**Cultural Notes:** French-speaking cultures place strong social importance on greetings — failing to greet someone (a shopkeeper, a neighbor, a colleague) before starting a conversation is considered impolite in most Francophone contexts. The *tu/vous* distinction reflects this same value: choosing the right form of address is a genuine social skill, not just a grammar rule.

**Prerequisites:** None. This is the learner's first chapter in FrancPrep.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can competently open and close a basic social interaction in French, in either a formal or informal setting, with a stranger or an acquaintance.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 1
**Lesson Title:** Basic Greetings (Bonjour/Salut/Bonsoir)
**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Recognize and use the core greeting and farewell expressions, matched correctly to time of day and formality.

**Estimated Time:** 20–25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
Think about how you greet people in your own language. Do you say something different in the morning than at night? Do you greet a stranger the same way you greet a close friend? Keep these questions in mind — French makes some of the same distinctions, and a few new ones.

---

# Lesson Explanation
French greetings change depending on two things: **the time of day** and **how well you know the person**.

- **Bonjour** ("hello" / "good day") is the all-purpose daytime greeting. It works with anyone, from a stranger to a close friend, from morning until early evening.
- **Bonsoir** ("good evening") replaces *bonjour* once evening arrives — roughly from the early evening onward.
- **Salut** ("hi" / "bye") is casual and is only used with people you know well — friends, close family, classmates. It is never used with a stranger or in a formal setting.
- **Au revoir** ("goodbye") is the standard way to say goodbye in any situation, formal or informal.
- **À bientôt** ("see you soon") is a warm, casual way to end a conversation when you expect to see the person again soon.
- **Bonne nuit** ("good night") is used only when someone is going to bed — not as a general evening farewell.

**Madame** (for a woman) and **Monsieur** (for a man) are polite titles often added after *bonjour* or *bonsoir* when addressing someone you don't know well: *Bonjour, Madame.*

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces only a small, fixed piece of the verb *être* ("to be"): the phrase pattern *Je suis...* ("I am...") used to state your name, which you'll need for the dialogues below. The full conjugation of *être* is built gradually across this chapter — you are not expected to master it yet.

**Formation (so far):**
- *Je suis* + [name] → "I am [name]."

**Usage:** This pattern is commonly used right after a greeting, when meeting someone for the first time: *Bonjour, je suis Claire.*

**Examples:**
1. Bonjour, je suis Antoine.
2. Bonsoir, je suis Madame Lefèvre.
3. Salut, je suis Léa !

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je suis à Claire.* → ✅ *Je suis Claire.* (No preposition is needed before a name with *être*.)
- ❌ Forgetting to capitalize proper names: *je suis claire* → ✅ *Je suis Claire.*

**Mini Drills:** Complete each sentence with *Je suis* and your own name.
1. Bonjour, __________.
2. Salut, __________ !

# LESSON 2

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Introducing Yourself
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Introduce yourself with your name using *je m'appelle*, and understand *être* extending to a third person (*il/elle est*).
**Grammar Focus:** *s'appeler* (to be called) — *je m'appelle*, *tu t'appelles*; *être* extended to *il/elle est*.
**Vocabulary Focus:** je m'appelle, comment tu t'appelles, enchanté(e), moi aussi, et toi, et vous
**Estimated Time:** 20–25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to say *Je suis [name]*. Today you'll learn a second, equally common way the French say their name — one you'll hear even more often in everyday conversation.

---

# Lesson Explanation
While *Je suis [name]* is correct, French speakers most commonly introduce themselves using **je m'appelle**, which literally means "I call myself" but simply translates as "my name is."

- **Je m'appelle Sophie.** — "My name is Sophie."
- **Comment tu t'appelles ?** — "What's your name?" (informal)
- **Enchanté** (said by a man) / **Enchantée** (said by a woman) — "Nice to meet you," said right after learning someone's name.
- **Moi aussi** — "Me too," a natural response when something applies to you as well.
- **Et toi ?** — "And you?" (informal) / **Et vous ?** — "And you?" (formal) — used to turn a question back to the other person.

We're also extending *être*: **il est** / **elle est** ("he is" / "she is") lets you talk about a third person, not just yourself.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| je m'appelle | my name is | zhuh mah-PELL | Je m'appelle Nora. |
| comment tu t'appelles | what's your name (informal) | koh-mahn tu tah-PELL | Comment tu t'appelles ? |
| enchanté / enchantée | nice to meet you | ahn-shahn-TAY | Enchantée, Madame ! |
| moi aussi | me too | mwah oh-SEE | Moi aussi, enchanté ! |
| et toi | and you (informal) | ay TWAH | Ça va, et toi ? |
| et vous | and you (formal) | ay VOO | Bonjour, et vous ? |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** *S'appeler* is a reflexive verb — literally, you "call yourself" a name. At A1, you only need two forms of it. We're also adding the third-person forms of *être*.

**Formation:**
| Pronoun | s'appeler | être |
|---|---|---|
| je | je m'appelle | je suis |
| tu | tu t'appelles | tu es |
| il / elle | il/elle s'appelle | il/elle est |

**Usage:** Use *je m'appelle* to state your own name; use *il/elle est* to describe who someone else is (often paired with a name): *Elle est Camille.*

**Examples:**
1. Je m'appelle Hugo. Et toi ?
2. Elle s'appelle Inès.
3. Il est content. (He is happy.) — a preview of how *être* connects to more than names.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Je m'appelle est Marc.* → ✅ *Je m'appelle Marc.* (No *est* needed with *s'appeler*.)
- ❌ *Il s'appelle il Paul.* → ✅ *Il s'appelle Paul.*

**Mini Drills:** Fill in with the correct form.
1. __________ (je m'appelle) Sarah.
2. __________ (il s'appelle) Antoine.

# LESSON 3

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 3
**Lesson Title:** Asking Someone's Name
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask someone's name in both informal and formal registers and understand the answer.
**Grammar Focus:** Question formation with *est-ce que* and rising intonation; *être* extended to *nous sommes* / *vous êtes*.
**Vocabulary Focus:** comment vous appelez-vous, qui est-ce, c'est, est-ce que
**Estimated Time:** 20–25 minutes

---

# Lesson Explanation
You already know *comment tu t'appelles ?* for informal situations. The formal version is **comment vous appelez-vous ?** — used with strangers, elders, or in professional settings.

French can form a yes/no question three ways at A1: rising intonation alone (*Tu es Marc ?*), or adding **est-ce que** to the front of a statement (*Est-ce que tu es Marc ?*). Both are common in speech; *est-ce que* is slightly more explicit and useful while you're still learning to hear intonation.

**Qui est-ce ?** means "Who is it / who is that?" and **c'est** ("it is / that is") is used to answer: *C'est Marc.*

We also complete more of *être*: **nous sommes** ("we are") and **vous êtes** ("you are," formal or plural).

---

# Warm-Up
Think of three ways you might ask someone's name in your own language depending on how formal the situation is. French makes exactly this same distinction.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| comment vous appelez-vous | what's your name (formal) | koh-mahn voo zah-play VOO | Comment vous appelez-vous, Madame ? |
| qui est-ce | who is it / that | kee ESS | Qui est-ce, là-bas ? |
| c'est | it is / that is | SEH | C'est Monsieur Dubois. |
| est-ce que | (question marker) | ess kuh | Est-ce que vous êtes Madame Roy ? |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson finishes the present tense of *être* and adds two ways to ask questions.

**Formation — Full Present Tense of Être (so far):**
| Pronoun | Être |
|---|---|
| je | suis |
| tu | es |
| il / elle | est |
| nous | sommes |
| vous | êtes |
| ils / elles | *(introduced next chapter)* |

**Usage:** *Est-ce que* + statement turns any statement into a question: *Vous êtes Madame Roy.* → *Est-ce que vous êtes Madame Roy ?*

**Examples:**
1. Est-ce que tu es Léa ?
2. Nous sommes en classe.
3. Vous êtes Monsieur Petit ?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Est-ce vous êtes Marc ?* → ✅ *Est-ce que vous êtes Marc ?* (Never drop *que*.)
- ❌ *Nous êtes* → ✅ *Nous sommes* (Don't mix up conjugation endings.)

**Mini Drills:**
1. Turn into a question using *est-ce que*: *Tu es Camille.* → __________
2. Complete: *Nous __________ amis.* (être)

# LESSON 4

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 4
**Lesson Title:** How Are You?
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask and answer "How are you?" appropriately in formal and informal contexts, and describe basic feelings.
**Grammar Focus:** *être* + adjective for describing feelings (*je suis fatigué(e)*); adjective agreement introduced minimally (masculine/feminine).
**Vocabulary Focus:** comment ça va, comment allez-vous, ça va bien/mal, fatigué(e), content(e), un peu
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Comment ça va ?** ("How's it going?") is the informal way to ask how someone is; **Comment allez-vous ?** is the formal equivalent. Both can be answered simply with **ça va** ("I'm okay / it's going well") or **ça va bien/mal** ("it's going well/badly").

To describe how you feel more specifically, French uses *être* + an adjective: **Je suis fatigué** (a man says) / **Je suis fatiguée** (a woman says) — "I am tired." Adjectives describing a female subject typically add an **-e** at the end; this is your first encounter with **adjective agreement**, a pattern you'll see constantly in French.

**Un peu** ("a little") softens a description: *Je suis un peu fatigué(e).*

---

# Warm-Up
"How are you?" is one of the most common exchanges in any language — but the answer isn't always a real answer! Think about how automatic your own "I'm fine, thanks" can be. French works the same way.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| comment ça va | how's it going (informal) | koh-mahn sah VAH | Salut ! Comment ça va ? |
| comment allez-vous | how are you (formal) | koh-mahn tah-lay VOO | Bonjour, comment allez-vous ? |
| ça va bien | it's going well | sah vah byan | Ça va bien, merci ! |
| ça va mal | it's going badly | sah vah MAHL | Ça va mal aujourd'hui. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** When *être* is followed by an adjective describing a person, the adjective usually changes form depending on whether the person is male or female — this is called **adjective agreement**. At A1, the most common pattern is simply adding **-e** for feminine.

**Formation:**
- Masculine: *Il est content.* / *Il est fatigué.*
- Feminine: *Elle est contente.* / *Elle est fatiguée.*

**Usage:** The extra **-e** does not change the pronunciation of *fatigué/fatiguée* but *does* change the pronunciation of *content/contente* (the final "t" becomes audible in the feminine form).

**Examples:**
1. Je suis content. (a man speaking)
2. Je suis contente. (a woman speaking)
3. Nous sommes un peu fatigués. *(plural agreement is previewed here only for recognition, not production)*

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ A woman saying *Je suis content.* → ✅ *Je suis contente.* (Match the adjective to your own gender.)
- ❌ *Je suis très fatigué mal.* → ✅ *Je suis très fatigué(e).* (Don't stack two feeling-words together.)

# LESSON 6

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 6
**Lesson Title:** Formal vs Informal (Tu vs Vous)
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Correctly identify and choose between *tu* and *vous* based on social context.
**Grammar Focus:** The *tu/vous* system explained explicitly and directly (consolidating and naming the pattern used implicitly since Lesson 1).
**Vocabulary Focus:** tu, vous, on se tutoie, on se vouvoie, quelqu'un que je connais bien, un(e) inconnu(e)
**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Lesson Explanation
Since Lesson 1, you've been using *tu* and *vous* without an explicit rule — this lesson names the pattern directly.

**Tu** is used with:
- Family and close friends
- Children
- People your own age in casual settings (increasingly common among younger people meeting for the first time)

**Vous** is used with:
- Strangers
- People older than you, or in a position of authority (a boss, a teacher, an official)
- Formal or professional settings, even with people you know

**On se tutoie ?** ("Shall we use *tu*?") is a common, friendly way to suggest switching from *vous* to *tu* once a relationship becomes closer — a real, natural social move in French, not just a grammar exercise.

**Un(e) inconnu(e)** means "a stranger" — and *vous* is the safe default with one.

---

# Warm-Up

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| tu | you (informal, singular) | TU | Tu es content ? |
| vous | you (formal, or plural) | VOO | Vous êtes Madame Roy ? |
| on se tutoie | shall we use "tu"? | ohn suh tu-TWAH | On se tutoie, si tu veux ! |
| on se vouvoie | we use "vous" (with each other) | ohn suh voo-VWAH | Au travail, on se vouvoie. |
| quelqu'un que je connais bien | someone I know well | kel-kuhn kuh zhuh kuh-neh byan | C'est quelqu'un que je connais bien. |
| un inconnu / une inconnue | a stranger | uhn nan-koh-NU | Ne parle pas à un inconnu. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson consolidates, rather than introduces, the *tu/vous* choice — the actual verb forms (*tu es* / *vous êtes*) were already taught in Lessons 1 and 3. What's new here is the explicit social rule governing when to use each.

**Formation (recap):**
| | tu-form | vous-form |
|---|---|---|
| être | tu es | vous êtes |
| s'appeler | tu t'appelles | vous vous appelez |

**Usage:** The choice is social, not grammatical — both forms are always "correct" French, but using the wrong one in the wrong context is a real social misstep, much like *Manifesto*-aligned FrancPrep content treats it: a genuine communicative skill, not just a rule to memorize.

**Examples:**
1. À un ami : Tu es fatigué ?
2. À un professeur : Vous êtes fatigué, Monsieur ?
3. On se tutoie ? — Oui, avec plaisir !

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ Using *tu* with a new boss on the first day → ✅ Default to *vous* until invited otherwise.
- ❌ Continuing to use *vous* with a close friend after years → generally sounds distant or overly formal, though not "wrong."

**Mini Drills:** Choose *tu* or *vous*.

# Grammar Summary (Full Consolidated Reference)

**Être (present tense, forms taught in this chapter):**
| Pronoun | Form |
|---|---|
| je | suis |
| tu | es |
| il / elle | est |
| nous | sommes |
| vous | êtes |

**S'appeler (forms taught in this chapter):**
| Pronoun | Form |
|---|---|
| je | m'appelle |
| tu | t'appelles |
| il / elle | s'appelle |

**Question formation:** *Est-ce que* + statement, or rising intonation alone.
**Adjective agreement (feminine, basic pattern):** add **-e** (*content → contente*; *fatigué → fatiguée*).
**Tu/vous:** *tu* for close/informal relationships; *vous* for strangers, elders, and formal/professional contexts.

# Mixed Practice Exercises (Comprehensive)

**1. Multiple Choice**
Which greeting fits 8:00 AM with a stranger?
a) Salut b) Bonjour c) Bonsoir d) Bonne nuit

**2. Matching**
1. Je m'appelle — a) It is / that is
2. C'est — b) My name is
3. Excusez-moi — c) Excuse me (formal)

**3. Fill in the Blank**
__________ (vous, être) Madame Aubert ?

**4. Sentence Ordering**
(a) Enchantée ! (b) Bonjour, je m'appelle Claire. (c) Je m'appelle Hugo, enchanté !

**5. Translation**
Translate into French: "Excuse me, Madam, what is your name?"

**6. Short Answer**
Explain, in your own words, the difference between *ça va bien* and *je suis content(e)*.

**7. Communicative Practice**
Record yourself (or say aloud to a partner) a full 30-second first-encounter scenario using at least six expressions from this chapter.

**Answer Key:**
1. b) Bonjour
2. 1-b, 2-a, 3-c
3. Êtes-vous *(or: Est-ce que vous êtes)*
4. b, c, a
5. Excusez-moi, Madame, comment vous appelez-vous ?
6. Sample answer: *Ça va bien* describes how things are going generally in the moment (a state), while *je suis content(e)* describes a specific personal feeling (happiness) tied to the speaker and agreeing with their gender.
7. (Open-ended — assess for range, register consistency, and correct use of *être*/*s'appeler*.)

# DELF A1-Style Mini-Assessment

**Section 1 — Listening Comprehension (based on Lesson 7's transcript):** Listen to (read) the café scene again and answer: Who uses *vous* with whom, and why, at each point in the conversation? *(3 points)*

**Section 2 — Reading Comprehension:** Read a new short passage:
*Bonsoir, Monsieur. Je m'appelle Inès. Comment allez-vous ? — Ça va bien, merci, un peu fatigué. Et vous ? — Ça va, merci !*

**English Translation:** *(A1–A2 support — hide behind a toggle in the platform UI)*
*Good evening, sir. My name is Inès. How are you? — I'm doing well, thank you, a little tired. And you? — I'm okay, thanks!*

Answer: (a) What time of day is it? (b) Is the exchange formal or informal? (c) How does the man describe how he feels? *(3 points)*

**Section 3 — Written Production:** Write a 4–6 line original dialogue introducing yourself to a stranger, asking their name, and asking how they are, in the formal register. *(4 points)*

**Section 4 — Oral Production (Self-Recorded or Partner-Assessed):** Introduce yourself aloud, state your name, and say how you are feeling today, using at least one adjective with correct agreement. *(2 points)*

**Answer Key — Sections 1–3:**
1. The waiter uses *vous* with Aline (a stranger); Léa and Aline use *tu* with each other (old friends); Aline uses *vous* with Julien (a stranger to her).
2. (a) Evening (*bonsoir*). (b) Formal (*vous*, *Monsieur*). (c) A little tired (*un peu fatigué*).
3. (Open-ended — assess against the Writing Checklist criteria used throughout the chapter: correct *vous* forms, appropriate courtesy expression, correct question formation.)
