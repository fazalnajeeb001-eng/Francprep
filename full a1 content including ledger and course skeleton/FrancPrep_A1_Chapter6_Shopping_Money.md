# Chapter 6 — Shopping & Money

---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to shop for everyday items, ask and understand prices, and handle simple transactions and complaints.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Identify common shops and what they sell.
- Ask and understand prices using numbers 21–100.
- Make a purchase and understand money vocabulary.
- Discuss sizes, colors, and preferences while shopping.
- Compare two products using descriptive language.
- Handle a simple return or complaint politely.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can name common shops and what they sell.
- I can ask and understand a price.
- I can make a purchase and understand basic money vocabulary.
- I can discuss sizes and colors when shopping.
- I can describe and compare two products.
- I can handle a simple return or complaint politely.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- **Demonstrative adjectives** (*ce, cet, cette, ces* — "this/that/these/those"), built progressively across the chapter.
- **Numbers 21–100**, split across two lessons: the regular pattern (21–69) and the historically irregular pattern (70–100), which genuinely works differently and deserves dedicated attention.

**Vocabulary Domains:** Shops, money, prices, clothing sizes (recycling Chapter 3's color vocabulary in a new context), returns/complaints.

**Cultural Notes:** Small, specialized shops (a dedicated *boulangerie* for bread, a *boucherie* for meat) remain a genuinely common part of daily life in France alongside supermarkets — not a nostalgic exaggeration, but a real, current pattern of how many people still shop for certain items.

**Prerequisites:** Chapters 1–5. This chapter assumes full command of *être*, *avoir*, adjective agreement, possessive adjectives, reflexive verbs, frequency adverbs, and the partitive/definite article system. It deliberately does **not** introduce comparative structures (*plus/moins/aussi... que*), which are reserved for Level A2.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can shop confidently for everyday items, understand and state prices up to 100, and handle a simple return.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# LESSON 1

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 1
**Lesson Title:** Shops & What They Sell
**Anchor Skill:** Reading (R)
**Lesson Objectives:** Identify common shops and what they sell, using the first demonstrative adjectives.
**Grammar Focus:** Demonstrative adjectives — introduction: *ce* (masculine) and *cette* (feminine), meaning "this/that."
**Vocabulary Focus:** un magasin, une boulangerie, une boucherie, un supermarché, une pharmacie, une librairie, vendre
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
Think about the different shops in your own neighborhood — a bakery, a pharmacy, a bookstore. Today you'll learn their French names, along with a new way to point at a specific one: "this" or "that."

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Un magasin** is the general word for "a shop." French has specific names for many specialized shops: **une boulangerie** (bakery), **une boucherie** (butcher's), **un supermarché** (supermarket), **une pharmacie** (pharmacy), **une librairie** (bookshop — note: this is a "false friend," it does *not* mean library, which is *une bibliothèque*).

**Ce** and **cette** are demonstrative adjectives — "this" or "that," depending on context. **Ce** is used before a masculine noun: **ce magasin** ("this shop"). **Cette** is used before a feminine noun: **cette boulangerie** ("this bakery").

**Vendre** ("to sell") is introduced here as a chunk (**elle vend**, **ils vendent**) — a regular *-re* verb, a new verb family whose full conjugation is reserved for a later level; for now, recognize and use these two forms.

---

|---|---|---|---|
| un magasin | a shop | uhn mah-gah-ZAN | Ce magasin est très grand. |
| une boulangerie | a bakery | ewn boo-lahn-zhuh-REE | Cette boulangerie vend du pain frais. |
| une boucherie | a butcher's shop | ewn boo-shuh-REE | Cette boucherie vend de la viande. |
| un supermarché | a supermarket | uhn sew-pehr-mar-SHAY | Ce supermarché vend des fruits et des légumes. |

| une librairie | a bookshop | ewn lee-brair-REE | Cette librairie vend des livres en français. |
| vendre *(chunk)* | to sell | VAHNDR | Cette boulangerie vend du pain. |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the first two forms of the demonstrative adjective.

**Formation (so far):**
| Gender | Demonstrative | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine | ce | ce magasin |
| Feminine | cette | cette boulangerie |

**Usage:** Context determines whether *ce/cette* means "this" or "that" — French doesn't distinguish them the way English does; the distinction, if needed, is added with a separate word (*-ci*/*-là*) at a later level.

**Examples:**
1. Ce magasin vend des livres.
2. Cette pharmacie est fermée aujourd'hui.
3. J'aime cette boulangerie — le pain est délicieux !

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Ce boulangerie* → ✅ *Cette boulangerie* (boulangerie is feminine).
- ❌ *Cette magasin* → ✅ *Ce magasin* (magasin is masculine).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: __________ (ce/cette) supermarché est grand.
2. Complete: __________ (ce/cette) pharmacie vend des médicaments.

# LESSON 2

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Asking Prices
**Anchor Skill:** Writing (W)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask and understand prices using numbers 21–69.
**Grammar Focus:** Numbers 21–69 — the regular pattern.
**Vocabulary Focus:** combien ça coûte, ça coûte, un euro, cher/chère, pas cher
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know numbers 0–20. Today you'll extend that all the way to 69 — and the good news is, from 21 onward, French numbers follow a clear, learnable pattern.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Combien ça coûte ?** ("How much does it cost?") is the essential shopping question. **Ça coûte** + a number + **euros** answers it: *Ça coûte vingt-cinq euros.*

Numbers 21–69 follow a regular pattern: the tens word (*vingt* = 20, *trente* = 30, *quarante* = 40, *cinquante* = 50, *soixante* = 60) plus the unit. For 21, 31, 41, 51, and 61, French inserts **et** ("and"): *vingt et un* (21), *trente et un* (31). For all other combinations, a hyphen simply joins the two: *vingt-deux* (22), *trente-cinq* (35).

**Cher/chère** ("expensive") and **pas cher** ("not expensive, cheap") describe prices.

---

# Vocabulary

|---|---|---|---|
| combien ça coûte | how much does it cost | kohm-byan sah KOOT | Combien ça coûte, s'il vous plaît ? |
| ça coûte | it costs | sah KOOT | Ça coûte trente euros. |
| un euro | a euro | uhn UH-roh | Ça coûte vingt euros. |
| cher / chère | expensive | SHEHR | Ce magasin est cher. |
| pas cher | not expensive / cheap | pah SHEHR | Ce pain n'est pas cher. |

| French | Number | French | Number |
|---|---|---|---|
| vingt et un | 21 | quarante | 40 |
| vingt-deux | 22 | quarante et un | 41 |
| trente | 30 | cinquante | 50 |
| trente et un | 31 | cinquante et un | 51 |
| trente-cinq | 35 | soixante | 60 |
| quarante | 40 | soixante et un | 61 |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the regular number pattern from 21 to 69, formally resolving the numbers previewed in earlier chapters.

**Formation:**
Tens word + (*et* + *un*, only for 1) OR + hyphen + digit (2–9).
- 21 = vingt **et** un / 22 = vingt-**deux** / 35 = trente-**cinq**

**Usage:** This same pattern was already used, unexplained, in Chapter 2 (*vingt-deux ans*) and Chapter 5 (*vingt-cinq euros*) — you can now fully understand and produce those numbers yourself.

**Examples:**
1. Ça coûte vingt-cinq euros.
2. J'ai trente et un ans. *(recalling Chapter 2's previewed pattern, now fully explained)*
3. Ce livre coûte quarante-trois euros.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Vingt-un* → ✅ *Vingt et un* (don't forget *et* for the "1" combinations).
- ❌ *Trente-et-deux* → ✅ *Trente-deux* (*et* is only used with 1, never with other digits).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Write out: 47 → __________
2. Write out: 21 → __________

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 3
**Lesson Title:** Making a Purchase
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)
**Lesson Objectives:** Make a purchase and understand money vocabulary, completing the number system to 100.
**Grammar Focus:** Numbers 70–100 — the irregular pattern.
**Vocabulary Focus:** l'argent, un billet, une pièce, la monnaie, je vais prendre
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
French numbers take a genuinely different turn after 69 — this isn't a small irregularity, it's a completely different counting logic. Today you'll learn it directly, so it never catches you by surprise.

---

# Lesson Explanation
From 70 onward, French counts differently: **soixante-dix** (70) is literally "sixty-ten," continuing as *soixante-onze* (71), *soixante-douze* (72), all the way to *soixante-dix-neuf* (79, "sixty-nineteen"). Then **quatre-vingts** (80) is literally "four-twenties," continuing as *quatre-vingt-un* (81), up to *quatre-vingt-dix* (90, "four-twenty-ten"), and *quatre-vingt-dix-neuf* (99, "four-twenty-nineteen"). Finally, **cent** (100).

This system has real historical roots (a base-20 counting pattern that survived in French while disappearing from most related languages) — it isn't random, but it does need direct memorization rather than pattern-guessing.

**L'argent** ("money"), **un billet** ("a bill/banknote"), **une pièce** ("a coin"), and **la monnaie** ("change," as in money given back) round out this lesson's shopping vocabulary. **Je vais prendre** ("I'll take...") is a useful chunk for finalizing a purchase.

---

# Vocabulary

|---|---|---|---|
| l'argent | money | lar-ZHAHN | Je n'ai pas beaucoup d'argent. |
| un billet | a bill/banknote | uhn bee-YEH | J'ai un billet de cinquante euros. |
| une pièce | a coin | ewn PYESS | J'ai une pièce d'un euro. |
| la monnaie | change (money given back) | lah moh-NEH | Voici votre monnaie, Madame. |
| je vais prendre *(chunk)* | I'll take | zhuh veh PRAHNDR | Je vais prendre ce livre. |

**Numbers 70–100:**

| French | Number |
|---|---|
| soixante-dix | 70 |
| soixante et onze | 71 |
| soixante-quinze | 75 |
| quatre-vingts | 80 |
| quatre-vingt-un | 81 |
| quatre-vingt-dix | 90 |
| quatre-vingt-quinze | 95 |
| cent | 100 |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson completes the number system from 70 to 100, with its distinct base-20 counting logic.

**Formation:**
- 70–79: *soixante* + (10–19): *soixante-dix* (70), *soixante-quinze* (75).
- 80–89: *quatre-vingts* + (0–9, hyphenated, no *et*): *quatre-vingt-un* (81), *quatre-vingt-cinq* (85).
- 90–99: *quatre-vingt-dix* + (0–9): *quatre-vingt-quinze* (95).
- 100: *cent*.

**Usage:** Note *quatre-vingts* takes an *-s* at exactly 80, but loses it when followed by another number (*quatre-vingt-un*, no -s) — a small but genuine spelling rule.

**Examples:**
1. Ce manteau coûte quatre-vingts euros.
2. J'ai soixante-quinze euros dans mon portefeuille.
3. Ça coûte quatre-vingt-dix-neuf euros — presque cent !

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Sept-dix* for 70 → ✅ *Soixante-dix* (French doesn't have a separate word for "seventy" — it's built from sixty.)
- ❌ *Quatre-vingt* (without -s) for exactly 80 → ✅ *Quatre-vingts* (the -s is required at exactly 80, only dropped when followed by another number).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Write out: 84 → __________
2. Write out: 100 → __________

# Warm-Up
You know *ce* and *cette*. Today you'll complete the demonstrative system — including a familiar-looking elision exception, and the plural form.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Cet** is used before a **masculine** noun that begins with a vowel sound — the same elision logic you've seen with *mon/ton/son*: **cet ordinateur** ("this computer"), not *"ce ordinateur."* **Ces** is the plural form for any gender: **ces chaussures** ("these shoes"), **ces pulls** ("these sweaters").

**Complete Demonstrative System:**
| Gender/Number | Form |
|---|---|
| Masculine singular | ce |
| Masculine singular, before a vowel sound | cet |
| Feminine singular | cette |
| Plural (any gender) | ces |

**La taille** ("size") uses the sizes you already know as physical descriptors from Chapter 3 — **petite, moyenne, grande** — now recycled in a clothing context. **Essayer** ("to try on") is introduced as a chunk: **je voudrais essayer**.

---

|---|---|---|---|
| la taille | the size | lah TIE | Quelle taille faites-vous ? |
| petit / petite (taille) | small (size) — *recycled from Ch3* | — | Je fais une taille petite. |
| moyen / moyenne | medium | mwah-YAN / mwah-YEN | Je fais une taille moyenne. |

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson completes the demonstrative adjective system.

**Formation (complete):**
| Gender/Number | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular | ce | ce pull |
| Masculine singular, vowel sound | cet | cet ordinateur |
| Feminine singular | cette | cette robe |
| Plural | ces | ces chaussures |

**Usage:** *Cet* applies purely for pronunciation ease, exactly like *mon/ton/son* before a vowel-sound noun — it does not indicate anything different in meaning from *ce*.

**Examples:**
1. Cet ordinateur est cher.
2. Ces chaussures sont très confortables.
3. Je voudrais essayer cette robe et ce pull.

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 6
**Lesson Title:** Returns & Simple Complaints
**Anchor Skill:** Listening (L)

**Estimated Time:** 25 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
No new grammar today — this lesson consolidates courtesy and polite-request patterns you already know, applied to a new, practical situation: returning something.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Rapporter** ("to bring back / return an item") is a useful chunk: **je voudrais rapporter ce pull** ("I'd like to return this sweater"). **Un problème** ("a problem") and **ça ne marche pas** ("it doesn't work") describe the issue. **Rembourser** ("to refund") completes the transaction: **je voudrais être remboursé(e)** ("I'd like to be refunded").
