---

# Chapter Overview

**Chapter Objective:** By the end of this chapter, learners will be able to identify city places, ask for and give directions, and navigate using public transportation and a map.

**Communicative Goals:**
- Identify common places in a town or city.
- Ask for directions politely.
- Give directions using the imperative.
- Describe the location of a place using prepositions.
- Use public transportation.
- Read and use a simple map.

**CEFR Can-Do Statements:**
- I can name common places in a city.
- I can ask for directions politely.
- I can give simple directions using the imperative.
- I can describe where something is located.
- I can use basic public transportation.
- I can read a simple map and find my way.

**Grammar Objectives:**
- **Prepositions of place**, split across two lessons: a first set (*dans, sur, sous, devant, derrière*) and a second, completing set (*à côté de, en face de, près de, loin de, entre*).
- **The imperative** (basic commands), split across two lessons: the formal *vous*-form (for asking a stranger) and the informal *tu*-form.

**Vocabulary Domains:** City places, directions, prepositions of place, public transportation, map-reading.

**Cultural Notes:** French towns are traditionally organized around a central square or church, often still the natural point of reference locals use when giving directions — "near the church" or "on the square" remains a genuinely common way people orient a stranger, even in an era of GPS.

**Prerequisites:** Chapters 1–7. This chapter assumes full command of *être*, *avoir*, demonstrative adjectives, and the *tu/vous* distinction, which becomes directly relevant to choosing the correct imperative form.

**Learning Outcomes:** A learner completing this chapter can ask for and follow directions confidently, using both prepositions of place and the imperative.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 3–3.5 hours across all eight lessons.

# Warm-Up
Think about the layout of your own town or city — where things are relative to each other. Today you'll learn the names of common city places, along with the first words French uses to describe where something is located.

---

# Lesson Explanation
Common city places: **une gare** (train station), **une banque** (bank), **un hôpital** (hospital), **une mairie** (town hall), **un parc** (park), **une église** (church), **une place** (a town square — a "false friend" for English "place," which is broader), **un pont** (a bridge).

Prepositions of place describe location: **dans** (in), **sur** (on), **sous** (under), **devant** (in front of), **derrière** (behind). *La banque est dans la rue principale* ("The bank is on the main street"), *le parc est derrière l'église* ("The park is behind the church").

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the first set of prepositions of place.

**Formation (so far):**
| French | English |
|---|---|
| dans | in |
| sur | on |
| sous | under |
| devant | in front of |
| derrière | behind |

**Usage:** These are simple, fixed prepositions that don't change form — no agreement needed, unlike adjectives.

**Examples:**
1. Le parc est devant la mairie.
2. La banque est derrière l'église.
3. Le chat est sous le pont.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ Confusing *dans* (in, inside) with *sur* (on, on top of) — genuinely different meanings, not interchangeable.
- ❌ *Une place* mistaken for "a place" in the general English sense → ✅ It specifically means a town square; the general concept of "a place" is *un endroit*, a different word.

**Mini Drills:**

2. Complete: La banque est __________ (behind) l'église.

# LESSON 2

# Lesson Information
**Lesson Number:** 2
**Lesson Title:** Asking for Directions
**Anchor Skill:** Speaking (S)
**Lesson Objectives:** Ask for directions politely, using the formal imperative.
**Grammar Focus:** The imperative — introduction: the formal *vous*-form for polite commands.
**Vocabulary Focus:** excusez-moi pour aller à, tournez, à droite, à gauche, tout droit
**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

---

# Warm-Up
You already know how to ask questions politely. Today you'll learn a new sentence type entirely — the imperative, used to give a command or instruction — starting with its polite, formal form.

---

# Lesson Explanation
**Excusez-moi, pour aller à... ?** ("Excuse me, how do I get to...?") is the standard way to ask for directions. The response typically uses the **imperative** — a command form that drops the subject pronoun entirely.

For **vous** (formal or plural), the imperative is identical to the present-tense *vous* form, simply without *vous* itself: *Vous tournez* → **Tournez !** ("Turn!"). This works for regular *-er* verbs directly, using the pattern you already know from Chapter 4.

**Tournez à droite** ("Turn right"), **tournez à gauche** ("Turn left"), **continuez tout droit** ("Continue straight ahead") are the core direction-giving phrases.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| excusez-moi, pour aller à | excuse me, how do I get to | ex-kew-zay mwah poor ah-lay ah | Excusez-moi, pour aller à la gare ? |
| tournez | turn (formal command) | toor-NAY | Tournez à droite. |
| à droite | (to the) right | ah DRWAHT | C'est à droite. |
| à gauche | (to the) left | ah GOHSH | Tournez à gauche. |
| tout droit | straight ahead | too DRWAH | Continuez tout droit. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the imperative mood, formal (*vous*) form.

**Formation:**
Present tense *vous* form, minus the subject pronoun *vous*: *Vous tournez.* → **Tournez !**

**Usage:** The formal imperative is the safe, polite default with a stranger — exactly the situation of asking for or giving directions to someone you don't know.

**Examples:**
1. Tournez à droite, puis continuez tout droit.
2. Excusez-moi, pour aller à la banque ?
3. Tournez à gauche devant l'église.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Vous tournez à droite* used as a command (keeping the subject pronoun) → ✅ *Tournez à droite !* (the pronoun is dropped in the imperative).
- ❌ Confusing *à droite* and *à gauche* — practice them together to avoid mixing them up under pressure.

**Mini Drills:**
1. Give the command "turn left" (formal): __________
2. Give the command "continue straight ahead": __________

**Estimated Time:** 25–30 minutes

# Warm-Up
You know the formal imperative. Today you'll learn its informal counterpart — useful when giving directions to a friend — plus a few more essential direction verbs.

---

# Lesson Explanation
The informal (*tu*) imperative is formed similarly to the formal one, but from the *tu* form of the verb — with one small twist for regular *-er* verbs: the final **-s** is dropped. *Tu tournes* → **Tourne !** (not *"Tournes !"*). This is a genuine spelling exception specific to *-er* verbs in the imperative.

**Continuez/continue** ("continue"), **traversez/traverse** ("cross"), and **prenez/prends** ("take," as in "take this street") complete the core set of direction verbs, each shown here in both formal and informal forms.

**La rue** ("the street") and **le carrefour** ("the intersection") round out this lesson's vocabulary.

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| continuez / continue | continue (formal/informal) | kohn-tee-NWAY / kohn-tee-NEW | Continue tout droit. |
| traversez / traverse | cross (formal/informal) | trah-vehr-SAY / trah-VEHRS | Traverse le pont. |
| prenez / prends | take (formal/informal) | pruh-NAY / PRAHN | Prends la première rue à droite. |
| la rue | the street | lah RU | La banque est dans cette rue. |
| le carrefour | the intersection | luh kar-foor | Tournez au carrefour. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson introduces the informal (*tu*) imperative and its one key spelling exception.

**Formation:**
| Verb | Vous (formal) | Tu (informal) |
|---|---|---|
| tourner | Tournez ! | Tourne ! *(no -s)* |
| continuer | Continuez ! | Continue ! *(no -s)* |
| traverser | Traversez ! | Traverse ! *(no -s)* |
| prendre *(irregular)* | Prenez ! | Prends ! |

**Usage:** The dropped -s applies specifically to regular *-er* verbs in the *tu*-imperative — a genuine, memorized exception, not derived from any other rule you've learned.

**Examples:**
1. Tourne à gauche, puis traverse le pont.
2. Prends la première rue à droite.
3. Continue tout droit jusqu'au carrefour.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *Tournes à gauche !* (keeping the -s) → ✅ *Tourne à gauche !* (drop the -s for *-er* verbs in the *tu*-imperative).
- ❌ Using *tu* imperative with a stranger → ✅ Stick with *vous* forms (Lesson 2) unless you know the person well.

**Mini Drills:**
1. Give the informal command "cross the bridge": __________
2. Give the informal command "take this street": __________

---

# Warm-Up
You know five prepositions of place from Lesson 1. Today you'll complete the system with five more — including a small but genuine grammar wrinkle involving *de*.

---

# Lesson Explanation
This lesson completes the prepositions of place: **à côté de** (next to), **en face de** (across from/facing), **près de** (near), **loin de** (far from), and **entre** (between — note: *entre* alone, no *de*).

The prepositions ending in **de** contract with a following definite article, just like you'd expect from earlier chapters: **à côté du parc** (*de* + *le* → *du*), **près de la banque** (*de* + *la* stays *de la*, no contraction needed), **en face de l'église** (*de* + *l'* → *de l'*, elision).

---

# Vocabulary

| French | English | Pronunciation Guidance | Example Sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| à côté de | next to | ah koh-tay duh | La banque est à côté du parc. |
| en face de | across from | ahn fahs duh | L'église est en face de la mairie. |
| près de | near | preh duh | Le pont est près de la gare. |
| loin de | far from | lwan duh | L'hôpital est loin d'ici. |
| entre | between | AHNTR | Le parc est entre la banque et l'église. |

---

# Grammar

**Explanation:** This lesson completes the prepositions of place, with attention to how *de*-based prepositions contract with the definite article that follows.

**Formation — Contraction Rules (recap, applied to new prepositions):**
| de + article | Becomes | Example |
|---|---|---|
| de + le | du | à côté du parc |
| de + la | de la (no change) | près de la banque |
| de + l' | de l' (elision) | en face de l'église |
| de + les | des | loin des magasins |

**Usage:** *Entre* is the one exception in this set — it never takes *de* at all: *entre la gare et la banque*, not *"entre de la gare."*

**Examples:**
1. La mairie est à côté du parc.
2. L'hôpital est loin de la gare.
3. Le pont est entre la place et l'église.

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ *À côté de le parc* → ✅ *À côté du parc* (contraction is required, not optional).
- ❌ *Entre de la gare et de la banque* → ✅ *Entre la gare et la banque* (never add *de* with *entre*).

**Mini Drills:**
1. Complete: La banque est __________ (next to, contracted) parc.
2. Complete: Le pont est __________ (between) la gare et la mairie.

# Lesson Explanation
**Une carte** means "a map" in this context — note this is the **same word** you learned in Chapter 5 for "the menu" (*la carte*)! Context always makes the meaning clear: a restaurant scene means "menu," a navigation scene means "map." This kind of double meaning (polysemy) is common in French, as in most languages.

**Il faut** + infinitive ("you must / you need to / it's necessary to") is introduced here as a useful fixed chunk for giving instructions: **il faut tourner à gauche** ("you need to turn left"). Its full grammatical behavior is addressed at a later level; for now, use it as a ready-made phrase.

**Se repérer** ("to find one's bearings / to get one's bearings") is a useful chunk for describing the general skill this lesson is about.
